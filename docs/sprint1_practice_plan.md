## How to Use This Document

This document drives your daily practice. The textbook explains why every element exists. This document tells you what to do today.

**Before your first session on any new concept:** read the corresponding textbook section. You need the why before the what makes full sense. But do not let reading substitute for practice. The textbook is reference. This document is your operating manual.

**The structure:** Five sections, every day, same order. Total time: approximately 60 minutes. The timing is a guide — go slightly long on a section that is producing results, go slightly shorter on one that is not, but complete all five every session.

**Stage labels appear throughout:** When this document says "Week 1 is Stage 1 territory," it means errors are expected and correct. Do not stop because you are wrong. Wrong answers are data. Stage 2 means sometimes right. Stage 3 — which the gate requires — means always right at tempo without conscious attention. "I can do it slowly" is Stage 2. Do not mistake it for Stage 3.

**The workbook pages at the end** are templates for filling in by hand. Print them or copy them into a practice notebook. They are designed to be written on, not just read.

## The Daily Session — Sprint 1 Template

Every session follows this structure. The time allocations are specific to Sprint 1 — the descriptions below tell you exactly what belongs in each slot during this sprint.

| Section | Time | Sprint 1 Purpose |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| **S1 — Plogger Foundation** | 10 min | Isolated di-chord ear training: [3] and [4] only, Hear→Sing→Name workflow |
| **S2 — Integrated Work** | 18 min | Zone 1 location on both instruments + Rhythm Code grid introduction |
| **S3 — Song Work** | 14 min | Ain't No Sunshine — chords, simplified melody, feel the song |
| **S4 — Free Play** | 10 min | Zone 1 improvisation over Am — ear-led, no rules except stay in zone |
| **S5 — The Synthesizer** | 8 min | All four chambers simultaneously — melody, harmony, voicings, rhythm at once |

**The session runs front to back. Do not skip sections and do not reverse the order.** S1 primes the ear. S2 connects ear to hands. S3 puts it in the song. S4 lets you own what you just practiced. S5 integrates everything. Each section feeds the next.

**Rules that apply to every section, every session:**
- Hear it before you play it. If you are about to play a [3], hear the interval in your head first.
- Sing what you play. Not performance singing — quiet, under your breath is fine. The act of producing an interval with your voice encodes it differently than just playing it.
- Mistakes are data. Stop at them only long enough to register what went wrong. Then keep going.

## Week 1 — First Contact

**Theme:** Open your ears. Learn the system. First contact with everything.

**What to expect:** This week everything is Stage 1 — Discovering. You will make errors constantly on the di-chord discrimination. Your chord shapes will buzz and mute. You will lose track of the rhythm grid. This is exactly right. Do not rush past Stage 1. First contact means first contact.

**Before your first session this week:** Read textbook Parts 1 (Musical OS — all of it), 4 (Voicings — guitar and piano sections), and 5 (Rhythm). These are the conceptual foundations for what S1, S2, and S3 ask you to do this week.

---

### S1 — Plogger Foundation (10 min) — Week 1

**Objective this week:** First contact with [3] and [4] as distinct sounds. Establish the Hear→Sing→Name workflow as a habit.

**What [3] and [4] sound like:**
- [3] is the minor third. It is modal (smooth, flowing, about 4 beats per second of acoustic pulsation), refers downward toward the fundamental, and is non-harmonic (dark, closed). The two notes of a minor third attract each other. Minor chords sound minor because of the [3] between the root and third.
- [4] is the major third. Same pulsation and shadow direction as [3] — they are twins in two out of three properties. The only difference: [4] is harmonic (light, open). The upper note belongs to the lower note's overtone series. Notes of a major third push slightly apart — open, expanding. Major chords sound major because of the [4].

To distinguish them reliably, you must listen to the closed vs. open quality — the color, not just the pitch distance.

**Step-by-step for Week 1 S1 (do this in order every day):**

1. Sit at the piano. Play A and C together — just those two notes, played simultaneously. Let them ring for five full seconds. Do not name it yet. Just receive the sound. Feel the closed, slightly heavy quality. That is [3].

2. Now play A and C# together — A and the black key immediately to the right of C. Let it ring for five seconds. Feel the difference: slightly more open, less contracted. That is [4].

3. Go back to A and C. Hear [3] again. Then A and C#. Hear [4]. Alternate back and forth five times, pausing five seconds on each. You are not analyzing. You are learning to recognize these sounds the way you would learn to recognize two different people's voices.

4. Now the singing step. Play A alone on piano. Hold it, or play it repeatedly. Now sing C above it — the [3] above A. Do not try to find C by name — find it by feel, by reaching upward until the interval feels contracted and minor. If you land wrong (too high or too low), that is fine. Adjust until the [3] quality clicks into place, and then hold the sung note.

5. Play A alone again. Now sing C# above it — the [4]. Reach upward and find the slightly more open, brighter version of the interval. The difference in your singing is small — C and C# are only a half step apart — but the sound quality shifts noticeably.

6. For the last two minutes: alternate randomly. Play A, then play either C or C# (choose randomly — flip between them). Before listening to what you played, try to identify whether it was [3] or [4] by the sound alone. Then sing the interval back. Then name it. This is the Hear→Sing→Name sequence.

**What "good" looks like vs. what "stuck" looks like:**

Good: You are wrong sometimes. You confuse [3] and [4] and then hear the difference when you compare them. You can sing both intervals imprecisely but recognizably. The Hear→Sing→Name sequence feels awkward but executable.

Stuck: You skip the hearing step and go straight to analyzing note names. Or you sing but do not actually listen to whether the interval quality matches what you are trying to produce. If you catch yourself skipping the five-second listening pause, slow down. The pause is the practice.

**Reps this week:** No formal counting of trials in Week 1. Your goal is ten sessions of this exercise — approximately two weeks — not a score. The score comes in Week 2.

### S2 — Integrated Work (18 min) — Week 1

**Objective this week:** Locate Zone 1 notes (A, C, E) on both instruments. Understand the 8-position grid conceptually and feel it physically.

**Exercise 1: Zone 1 Location (9 min)**

Zone 1 for Am contains exactly three notes: A (root), C (third), E (fifth). Your task this week is to find these three notes on both guitar and piano, play each one slowly, and sing the di-chord interval between them.

*On guitar:*

Find and play each Zone 1 note in open position. Here is where they live:

- **A** lives on: the open 5th string (A), the 2nd fret of the 4th string (D string, 2nd fret = E... wait — actually D string: open=D, 2nd fret = E, so A is at 2nd fret of the 3rd string G... let's be precise). In Am open position: the open 5th string is A. The 2nd fret of the 3rd string (G string) is also A.
- **C** lives on: the 1st fret of the 2nd string (B string, 1st fret = C).
- **E** lives on: the open 1st string (high E), and the open 4th string (D string... wait — open D string = D). Let us be exact using standard tuning (E-A-D-G-B-E from 6th to 1st string):
  - String 6 = E (open), String 5 = A (open), String 4 = D (open), String 3 = G (open), String 2 = B (open), String 1 = E (open)
  - In the Am chord: string 5 open = A (root), string 4 at 2nd fret = E (fifth), string 3 at 2nd fret = A (root), string 2 at 1st fret = C (third), string 1 open = E (fifth). Do not play string 6.

Play each note individually, one at a time, naming it as you play: "A... C... E..." Then play them in sequence, ascending and descending: A-C-E-C-A. Then play just A and C together (that is [3] — the same interval you trained in S1). Then play A and E together (that is a [7] — a perfect fifth; you will learn this properly in Sprint 2, but feel it now: stable and open). Then strum the full Am chord and hear all three Zone 1 notes ringing at once.

*On piano:*

Zone 1 for Am on piano is the span A-C-E, starting from any A. Use the A just above middle C (which sits just to the left of the group of three black keys in the center of the keyboard; Middle C is the C immediately to the left of that group of two black keys near the center).

- **A** is three white keys to the right of Middle C (C-D-E-F-G-A — count up six white keys from Middle C, or find the A that sits just to the right of the group of three black keys in that octave).
- **C** is two white keys above A (A-B-C).
- **E** is two white keys above C (C-D-E).

Play A-C-E slowly: A with your right index finger, C with your right ring finger (or middle finger — find the most comfortable reach), E with your right pinky (or ring). Let each note ring. Then play A and C simultaneously: hear [3]. Then play A and E simultaneously: hear the [7] fifth. Then play all three at once: that is Am in its simplest piano voicing.

*Crossing instruments:*

After doing this on each instrument separately, do one crossing exercise: play A on guitar (open 5th string), find the same A on piano (the note that sounds identical in pitch), and play them at the same time. Then C, then E. Confirm that the same note sounds the same across both instruments. The di-chord is not an instrument property — it lives in the relationship between the pitches.

**Exercise 2: 8-Position Grid Introduction (9 min)**

Read this before doing it: The 8-position grid divides a measure of 4/4 time into eight equal slots, each one eighth note long. Position 1 is the downbeat — beat one, the heaviest and most stable. Positions 3, 5, 7 are beats two, three, four. The even positions (2, 4, 6, 8) are the "ands" between beats.

*Part A — Feel the grid with your hands (4 min):*

1. Set a metronome or drum loop to 80 BPM (beats per minute). If you do not have a metronome, tap your foot steadily.
2. Clap on every beat: positions 1, 3, 5, 7. This is four quarter notes per measure. Count out loud as you clap: "ONE, two, THREE, four" — positions 1 and 5 (beats 1 and 3) get more emphasis.
3. Now clap on every position: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8. This is all eighth notes — twice as many claps at the same BPM. Feel how the eighth notes subdivide the beat.
4. Go back to clapping only positions 1 and 5 — just the downbeats of beats one and three. Let the other positions pass in silence. Feel the space between claps. That space is not emptiness — it is the grid continuing silently.
5. Now clap only positions 1 and 5, but say the other position numbers out loud while they pass: clap "ONE," say "two," clap "THREE," say "four," repeat. You are tracking all eight positions while only sounding two.

*Part B — The Ain't No Sunshine "I know" rhythm (5 min):*

The famous stop-time section of Ain't No Sunshine is built on silence. Each "I know" lands approximately on position 1, then goes silent. The silence lasts about two beats before the next "I know."

Play Am on guitar or piano. Strum or play the chord once on position 1, then let it ring and count silently: "two, three..." Wait about two beats, then play again on the next position 1. You are playing the chord on position 1 of every other measure — one strike, then silence, then another strike.

Say "I know" rhythmically with each chord strike. Feel how the silence after "I know" is not a pause waiting to be filled — it is a rhythmic event, a "stop," and the next "I know" arrives exactly where the grid expects it. That expectation — created by the grid and fulfilled by the stop — is what makes the groove.

This is a stop. You will use this concept throughout the sprint.

### S3 — Song Work (14 min) — Week 1

**Objective this week:** Learn the three chords of Ain't No Sunshine by shape and sound. Feel the song as music, not as an exercise.

**Before playing anything:** Listen to a recording of Ain't No Sunshine twice. Do not play along. Just listen. Notice:
- When does the chord change?
- Where is the lowest note at each chord change?
- Where is the stop-time "I know" section?
- Can you feel beat one of each measure?

You do not need to answer these questions analytically. Just listen with them in your mind.

**The chord structure of Ain't No Sunshine:**

The song uses three chords: Am, G, and Em (some arrangements use Dm instead of Em — either is fine for Sprint 1; use whichever sounds right with your recording). The chord sequence in the verse section is approximately:

| Measure | Chord | What it sounds like |
|---------|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | Am | Minor tonic — home in a minor world |
| 3 | G | A momentary brightening — not major-home, just brighter |
| 4 | Em | Back to minor, different shade |
| (repeat) | Am | Home again |

The stop-time "I know" section stays almost entirely on Am. After the "I know" section, the song returns to the verse structure.

**What to do this week in S3:**

*Day 1 and 2:*

Guitar only. Hold Am in open position. Strum it once — just one strum — on beat one of each measure. Do not worry about a strumming pattern. Do not think about fingering beyond getting the chord to ring cleanly. Your one task: play Am, let it ring, feel when beat one arrives, play it again. Do this for 4 minutes.

Then add G. Strum Am, wait a measure (or however long the song stays on Am), then move to G. Play one strum of G, then move back to Am. The chord change should feel smooth — if your hand is fumbling for the G shape, go slower. The change itself is the practice.

Then add Em. Now you can move through Am → G → Em → Am in a slow, one-strum-per-chord sequence.

*Day 3 and 4:*

Put on the recording. Play along at one strum per chord. You will be slower than the recording — that is fine. Your goal is to track the chord changes by ear, not to keep up rhythmically. When the recording moves to G, you move to G. Follow the harmony.

*Day 5 and beyond:*

Attempt to match the basic feel of the recording: Am gets more beats than G and Em. Try to feel when the chord wants to change. Do not count — listen. The song will tell you.

Piano version (add this once guitar is solid for the week, or alternate days):

Play the root note of each chord with your left hand (A for Am, G for G, E for Em), and play the three Zone 1 notes with your right hand. Root in the bass, chord tones on top. Just hold each chord until the harmony changes. No rhythm yet — just tracking the changes.

**What "good" looks like vs. what "stuck" looks like:**

Good: Chord changes produce sound, even if buzzy. You can feel when the harmony moves even if you are slightly late. The song feels like the song.

Stuck: You are stopping completely to refinger the chord, losing the beat entirely, and then wondering where you are in the song. If this is happening, slow down further. Slower than feels useful. The chord shape must be available before the change arrives — you need to be there a beat early, not scrambling.

### S4 — Free Play (10 min) — Week 1

**Objective this week:** Hear what you own. Play only Zone 1 notes of Am (A, C, E) without a rhythmic constraint, on whichever instrument feels more natural today.

**The rules:**
- Stay on A, C, and E only. No other notes this week.
- No prescribed rhythm. Play at whatever pace feels natural.
- Do not try to sound musical. Do try to listen to every note as it sounds.

**What to do:**

Pick up the guitar or sit at the piano. Choose one instrument for this section today. (Next session, choose the other.)

Play A. Hold it. Hear it ring. Then move to C. Hear the [3] quality — the closed, minor-third character of the step from A to C. Then move to E. Hear the [7] quality — the stable, open fifth sound. Then move back down: E to C (a [3] downward — different quality than [3] upward), C to A.

After a couple minutes of slow exploration, begin to vary the order. Go A-E-C-A. Go C-A-E. Go E-C-A-C-E. There is no wrong sequence. There is only: are you hearing each interval as it arrives?

At some point in the 10 minutes, begin to feel a subtle pulse — even just the sense that some moments feel more stable and some less. Let that feeling guide when you land on A versus when you move away. Do not count. Do not impose a rhythm. Just listen for the pull of A as home.

**This is not performance. It is ear-led exploration.** You are learning what these three notes sound like in relation to each other. You are building the sensory map of Am's Zone 1.

### S5 — The Synthesizer (8 min) — Week 1

**Objective this week:** Run all four chambers simultaneously — briefly, simply, imperfectly. This is your first experience of integration. It will feel like too much. That is expected.

**The four chambers in 8 minutes:**

Set up at whichever instrument is most comfortable. Play the Ain't No Sunshine chord loop: Am → G → Em (or Am → G → Dm) → Am. Repeat this loop for 8 minutes.

While you play:
- **Melody (Chamber 1):** Hum or sing the root of each chord as it arrives. Am arrives: hum A. G arrives: hum G. Em arrives: hum E. Keep the hum quiet — it is under your breath, but your vocal cords must be producing it. Do not stop humming when the chord changes; slide to the new root.
- **Harmony (Chamber 2):** As each chord arrives, say its name in your head (or whisper it): "Am... G... Em..." You are naming the harmony as you hear it.
- **Voicings (Chamber 3):** Play the chord shapes you have been learning. Guitar open-position chords or piano Zone 1 three-note voicings. The shape you play is the voicing.
- **Rhythm (Chamber 4):** Feel the pulse. Position 1 of each measure — beat one — should feel heavier than the others. Let that landing feel guide your chord strikes.

**All four chambers are running at once.** Melody in your voice, harmony in your mind, voicings in your hands, rhythm in your body. This is The Synthesizer — the moment of integration.

It will feel like too much this week. Run it anyway. Eight minutes. Do not stop.

**What "good" looks like this week:** You are doing all four things imperfectly at once. Chords are slightly buzzy, humming falls slightly off pitch, rhythm is unsteady. This is Stage 1 of synthesis. It is correct.

**What to notice:** Is there any moment — even a single measure — where it stops feeling like work and starts feeling like music? Notice that moment. That is what you are working toward.

## Week 2 — Building Ears

**Theme:** [3] and [4] becoming reliable. Ain't No Sunshine starting to feel like music, not exercises.

**What to expect:** By the end of Week 2, you should be getting [3] vs. [4] right most of the time (15/20 or better on the tracking log). Guitar chord changes should happen without stopping. The 8-position grid should be physically internalized — you can feel eight subdivisions without counting.

**Before this week:** No new textbook reading required. Review the Pictograph section of textbook Part 1 if you have not yet studied the visual encoding of [3] and [4].

---

### S1 — Plogger Foundation (10 min) — Week 2

**Objective this week:** Move from "sometimes distinguishing" to "usually distinguishing" [3] vs. [4]. Run 20 formal trials per session and track your score.

**How to run 20 trials (use Workbook Page 2 to track):**

1. Sit at the piano. Have your workbook open to the Di-Chord Ear Log.
2. Each trial: play two notes simultaneously — either A and C (which is [3]) or A and C# (which is [4]). Choose randomly. One way to randomize: look away from the keyboard before playing, reach for the two-note span, and commit without looking to see which you landed on.
3. Before naming it: hear it for three seconds. Let the quality register.
4. Name it: "[3]" or "[4]."
5. Then look at what you actually played. Record: correct or incorrect.
6. Do not adjust your playing to be more "fair." If you accidentally played [3] twice in a row, play [4] on the next trial. The point is 20 trials, not 10 of each.

After 20 trials, count your score. What the scores mean:
- **10/20 or below:** You are guessing. Do not be discouraged — this is still Stage 1. The solution is more listening time before naming. Take five full seconds to hear the interval before you identify it.
- **11-14/20:** You are beginning to hear the difference but it is not yet reliable. This is normal Stage 2 territory for early Week 2. Keep going.
- **15-18/20:** Good progress. You are hearing [3] vs. [4] as distinct qualities most of the time. Stage 2 solidifying.
- **19-20/20:** Stage 3 approaching. Do not declare victory yet — run the trials three days in a row at 19-20 before trusting it. One good day can be luck.

**Adding the Pictograph this week:**

After your 20 trials, spend 2 minutes on the Pictograph. Draw the glyph for [3]: a rounded outline (smooth, because modal pulsation), shadow to the left (refers down toward the fundamental), filled dark (non-harmonic, closed). Then draw the glyph for [4]: rounded outline, shadow to the left, but this time light (harmonic, open). The only difference between the two pictographs is the color. Let that visual difference reinforce the acoustical difference — both modal, both referring downward, but one closed and one open.

### S2 — Integrated Work (18 min) — Week 2

**Objective this week:** Both instruments simultaneously. Zone 1 on guitar while finding the same notes on piano. Begin mapping the Ain't No Sunshine rhythm to the 8-position grid.

**Exercise 1: Zone 1 Across Both Instruments (9 min)**

This week you are connecting the same notes across both instruments in real time.

Play A on the guitar's open 5th string. While it is ringing, find A on the piano with your left hand (or right hand if left is unavailable). You are hearing the same pitch produced by two different physical systems. Play them at the same time. Notice: the note is the same. The instrument is different. The di-chord is neither guitar nor piano — it is the pitch relationship itself.

Now: play C on guitar (1st fret of the 2nd string). Find C on piano. Play them together.

Now: play E on guitar (open 1st string). Find E on piano. Play together.

After doing A, C, E individually, play the Zone 1 sequence on guitar (A-C-E ascending) and follow with your piano hand one note behind: A on guitar, then A on piano, then C on guitar, then C on piano, then E on guitar, then E on piano. Not simultaneously this time — a call-and-response between instruments.

Then reverse: piano leads, guitar follows. The note from one instrument rings until the matching note from the other responds.

Finally: play Am as a chord on guitar (all strings) and Am as a three-note block chord on piano simultaneously. Listen to the total sound — both instruments, all Zone 1 notes, all at once.

*This exercise serves three purposes simultaneously:* (1) ear training — confirming that the same notes sound like themselves across instruments; (2) Zone 1 fluency — finding A, C, E without searching; (3) instrument independence — the hands can work separately while the ear monitors both.

**Exercise 2: Mapping the "I Know" Section to the Grid (9 min)**

By now you can feel the 8-position grid and you know what the stop-time section of Ain't No Sunshine sounds like. This exercise connects them.

Draw or write out eight boxes in a row — your 8-position grid. Label them 1 through 8. This represents one measure.

The "I know" section works approximately like this: position 1 is active (the "I" syllable), positions 2 through 8 are silent (the stop), then position 1 of the next measure is active again ("know"). Some performances place both "I" and "know" in a single measure; others spread them over two measures. For this exercise, use the simpler version: one strike per measure, landing on position 1.

Play Am on guitar or piano. Strike it once on position 1. Count silently through positions 2-8 while the chord rings and decays. Feel position 1 of the next measure approaching — and land on it cleanly. Say "I know" with each strike.

After four measures, add the following variation: anticipate position 1 by landing on position 8 of the previous measure instead. This means you play the chord one eighth note early — before beat one arrives — which is an anticipation. Now say "I know" with each anticipation landing. Feel the difference: landing directly on position 1 feels declarative, like a statement. Landing on position 8 (anticipation) feels like leaning in — forward momentum.

Spend 5 minutes exploring these two versions. Then try to play what feels closest to Bill Withers' actual performance. His timing has natural variation — that variation is part of the groove.

### S3 — Song Work (14 min) — Week 2

**Objective this week:** Add a basic strumming pattern on guitar. Aim for clean chord changes, two per measure when the song moves.

**The basic strumming pattern for this week:**

Play down strums on positions 1 and 5 — beat one and beat three of each measure. That is two downstrokes per measure. This is the skeleton of almost every groove in 4/4 time.

Count (or feel) position 1: strum down. Hold through positions 2, 3, 4. Feel position 5: strum down again. Hold through 6, 7, 8. Repeat.

This is not the full Ain't No Sunshine groove yet. It is the rhythmic backbone. Once this feels automatic — once you do not have to think about when to strum — you will add the anticipation strums. Not yet.

**This week's song work sessions:**

*Days 1-2:* Guitar only. Play the Am-G-Em loop with down strums on 1 and 5. The chord change from Am to G should happen before the strum — have your left hand already in the G shape before your right hand strums it. Anticipate every change.

*Days 3-4:* Play the song along with a recording. When you lose your place, do not restart from the beginning — wait, find beat one, and re-enter. Losing your place and re-entering is a more valuable skill than stopping to restart cleanly.

*Day 5:* Piano only. Left hand plays the root note of each chord (A for Am, G for G, E for Em) on beat one. Right hand plays Zone 1 of the current chord (the three notes A-C-E for Am, G-B-D for G, E-G-B for Em). No strumming pattern on piano — just strike the notes and hold. Let the chord sustain. Move to the next chord when the music moves.

**Piano Zone 1 notes for each chord:**

| Chord | Root (left hand) | Zone 1 right hand |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Am | A | A - C - E |
| G | G | G - B - D |
| Em | E | E - G - B |

**What "good" looks like vs. what "stuck" looks like:**

Good: Chord changes are clean at least half the time. You can feel beat one without counting. The song sounds like the song.

Stuck: The chord change causes a complete stop in the strumming pattern every time. If this is happening: remove the strumming pattern entirely and just change chords in time, one note per chord, until the change itself is fluent. Add the strum back only once the change does not break your momentum.

### S4 — Free Play (10 min) — Week 2

**Objective this week:** Zone 1 improvisation over Am-G-Em vamp, with singing.

**The setup:** On guitar, set up a loop if you have a looper pedal, or ask someone to strum Am-G-Em for you, or use a backing track. If none of these are available, play the Am-G-Em loop with your fretting hand while using only open strings for melody — which is limiting but workable. Alternatively, do this exercise on piano: left hand plays Am-G-Em one chord per measure, right hand improvises Zone 1 notes.

**What to do:** Improvise using only Zone 1 notes. But this week, do not stay on Am's Zone 1 when the chord changes. When G arrives (G-B-D), move to G's Zone 1 notes. When Em arrives (E-G-B), move to Em's Zone 1.

You do not need to switch notes perfectly on the chord change — a delayed response is fine. The goal is that you are tracking the harmony with your melodic choices: Am sounds like Am notes, G sounds like G notes.

**Sing what you play.** Every note you play on the instrument, produce that same pitch with your voice. Quiet humming is fine. This forces your ear to monitor whether the note you are singing matches the note you are playing. If they diverge, you will hear it — and that discrepancy is the ear training.

### S5 — The Synthesizer (8 min) — Week 2

**Objective this week:** Synthesis with root movement naming. As each chord arrives, say the root movement from the previous chord.

The root movements in the Ain't No Sunshine loop:
- Am to G: the root moves from A down to G — that is a [2] (whole step down). You have not formally learned [2] yet (that is Sprint 4), but you can hear it.
- G to Em: the root moves from G down to E — that is a [3] (minor third down). You know [3].
- Em to Am: the root moves from E up to A — that is a [5] (perfect fourth up). You have not formally learned [5] yet, but it sounds open and resolved.

You do not own these movements yet. Sprint 1 is the orientation sprint — no harmonic movement gate. What you are doing this week is hearing them for the first time in context. When Am arrives, say "Am." When G arrives, say "G... that was a step down." When Em arrives, say "Em... that was a minor third down." Do not stress about being correct — you are noticing, not testing.

Run the chord loop for 8 minutes. Melody in your voice (hum the root), harmony named aloud (chord name and movement), voicings in your hands, rhythm felt in your body. Imperfect and alive.

## Week 3 — Consolidation

**Theme:** Stage 2 solidifying toward Stage 3. [3]/[4] discrimination should be 18/20 or better. Ain't No Sunshine should be playable on both instruments in simplified form.

**What to expect:** This week the work gets harder because the standards go up. Week 1 asked for first contact. Week 2 asked for progress. Week 3 asks for consistency. If [3]/[4] is not reaching 18/20, this week's S1 is the diagnostic and the prescription.

---

### S1 — Plogger Foundation (10 min) — Week 3

**Objective this week:** [3] and [4] in melodic context. Find them inside a major scale.

**The [3] and [4] inside a major scale:**

You know the sound of a major scale — do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do. The intervals between the notes are not all the same. Some are whole steps ([2]), and some are half steps ([1]). But the scale also contains [3] and [4] within it — between non-adjacent notes.

Play C major on piano: C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C. Now play just C and E together. That is [4] — the major third, the di-chord that makes C major sound major. Now play C and Eb (C and the black key between D and E — on the C major scale, use a chromatic note here). That is [3] — the minor third.

Now play E and G together (both in C major, white keys). That is [3] — the minor third between the 3rd and 5th of the scale. Play C and E ([4]), then E and G ([3]). The major triad contains both: a [4] from the root to the 3rd, and a [3] from the 3rd to the 5th.

Minor chord: play A and C ([3]) then C and E ([4]). Am contains a [3] from root to 3rd, and a [4] from 3rd to 5th. The minor chord is the inversion of the major chord's di-chord order: [3]+[4] from the bottom vs. [4]+[3] from the bottom.

**This week's S1 exercise (7 min):**

Play a note on piano — any white key. Sing the [4] above it. Then play it and check. Now sing the [3] above the same note. Then play it and check. Do this from five different starting notes.

Then reverse: play a note. Sing the [3] below it, then the [4] below it. Check by playing.

The goal is: [3] and [4] from any starting note, in either direction, without needing to find them by counting. If you have to count semitones (A... A#... B... C... there, that's [3]), you are not at Stage 3 yet. The target is: hear the note, produce the interval, arrive at it through sound quality rather than counting.

**Score your 20 trials today.** Target: 18/20. If you are below 15, you have not yet crossed into Stage 2 territory and need more listening time in the exercise — spend 8 of your 10 minutes on pure listening without naming, and run only 10 trials with names.

---

### S2 — Integrated Work (18 min) — Week 3

**Objective this week:** Both instruments simultaneously — guitar chord while piano melody, or vice versa.

**Exercise 1: Guitar Chord + Piano Melody (9 min)**

This requires you to have both instruments accessible at the same time. If your setup allows: sit at the piano with your guitar resting accessible on a stand. Or sit with the guitar in playing position close to a keyboard.

Play Am chord on guitar with your left hand. While it rings, play Zone 1 melody (A-C-E in sequence) with your right hand on piano. You are holding a guitar chord shape with one side of your body and moving through piano melody with the other.

Change to G chord on guitar. Right hand on piano finds G's Zone 1: G-B-D.

Change to Em chord on guitar. Right hand on piano finds Em's Zone 1: E-G-B.

If this physical setup is not possible (instruments are in different rooms or on different floors), alternate between instruments within the exercise: 4 minutes of guitar Am chord + voice singing Zone 1 melody (voice substitutes for the piano hand), then switch and play piano Am chord with left hand while right hand plays melody.

**Exercise 2: Full Song Integration (9 min)**

Play Ain't No Sunshine on both instruments in sequence — not simultaneously, but back to back in the same session.

First pass (4 min): Guitar. Chord changes with your Week 2 strumming pattern (positions 1 and 5). No stopping for mistakes. All the way through the verse and "I know" section.

Second pass (4 min): Piano. Left hand roots (A, G, E on beats one of each measure), right hand Zone 1 melody (improvising from the Zone 1 notes of each chord — not a fixed melody, but notes from the chord as it goes by). Simplified and real.

One minute between passes: name what felt Stage 2 on guitar, name what felt Stage 2 on piano. Write it in your workbook. Those are your S2 drill targets for tomorrow.

---

### S3 — Song Work (14 min) — Week 3

**Objective this week:** Ain't No Sunshine on both instruments at simplified but playable level. The song should start to feel musical.

**Guitar:** This week add the forward strum — a light upstroke on position 6 (the "and" of beat 3) in addition to your downstrokes on 1 and 5. This creates a "down-down-up" feel within the measure:

- Position 1: down strum (strong)
- Position 5: down strum (medium)
- Position 6: up strum (light — just brush the top two or three strings)

This is not yet the full groove. It is the first rhythmic variation. Add it only once positions 1 and 5 are automatic.

Play the entire song — verse section plus "I know" section — without stopping. When you make a mistake, do not restart. Keep the pulse alive and re-enter.

**Piano:** Aim for a simplified version where:
- Left hand plays the root of each chord on beat one (and possibly beat three)
- Right hand plays one to two Zone 1 notes of the melody — not all three, not chords, just the top note of each chord (C for Am, B for G, B or G for Em)

Play this from start to finish without stopping. A simple but complete version.

**Week 3 standard:** You should be able to play the song on both instruments in a single session with no complete stops. Mistakes are fine. Stopping is not.

---

### S4 — Free Play (10 min) — Week 3

**Objective this week:** Both instruments if possible. If not, alternate every 2 minutes between them.

Play Zone 1 improvisation over the Am-G-Em loop (same as Week 2), but this week:

- When you are on guitar, try to find Zone 1 notes in more than one position on the neck. Am's Zone 1 (A, C, E) lives in multiple places beyond the open-position chord. A appears at the 5th fret of the low E string. E appears at the open 1st and 4th strings. C appears at the 5th fret of the 3rd string (or 8th fret of the 1st string).
- When you are on piano, move Zone 1 notes to a higher octave. Play A-C-E one octave up. Feel the same quality in a different register.

Sing what you play. Track the harmony by ear — let the chord change guide your note choice, not your predetermined plan.

---

### S5 — The Synthesizer (8 min) — Week 3

**Objective this week:** Full synthesis — all four chambers simultaneously for 8 minutes without interruption.

Set yourself up on one instrument (whichever is more comfortable) and run the Ain't No Sunshine progression:

- **Melody (Chamber 1):** Play Zone 1 notes with one hand (or voice). The melody does not need to be fixed — improvise from Zone 1 of whatever chord is currently playing.
- **Harmony (Chamber 2):** Name each chord aloud as it arrives — "Am... G... Em..." Include the root movement if you can hear it.
- **Voicings (Chamber 3):** The other hand (or the full chord grip on guitar) plays the chord shapes.
- **Rhythm (Chamber 4):** Feel the 8-position grid. Positions 1 and 5 are the anchors. Let your chord strikes land there.

Run 8 minutes. No stopping. The goal is not perfection — it is the experience of all four chambers running simultaneously without any one of them shutting down the others. If you lose the melody, keep the harmony going. If you lose the chord, keep the rhythm. The synthesis degrades gracefully; it does not collapse.

## Week 4 — Gate Approach

**Theme:** Are you ready? Honest self-assessment toward Stage 3. This week stress-tests what you think you own.

**What to expect:** Week 4 is not a harder practice week — it is an honest practice week. You are going to do the things you have been doing, but in new contexts, under mild pressure, and in a different key. Genuine Stage 3 means it works anywhere, not just in the familiar context where you practiced it.

---

### S1 — Plogger Foundation (10 min) — Week 4

**Objective this week:** [3] and [4] in random musical context — active listening in real music.

**The exercise:** Put on a song you do not know well. It does not need to be a practice-room song — it can be anything (except country, which the curriculum avoids). Anything with a clear melody.

Listen for thirty seconds. Then: can you identify any moment where the melody moves by a minor third ([3]) or a major third ([4])? You are not counting semitones. You are listening for the quality: the rounded, smooth character of both [3] and [4] compared to the step-like quality of [2] or the wide-open quality of [7].

Do not feel pressure to be right. This is active listening, not a test. If you hear something you think might be a [3] or [4], note it. Then go back and listen again — sing the interval you heard, and see if the quality matches.

**Why this matters:** Stage 3 means "can be identified by ear in a song in real time, without preparation." This exercise is the Stage 3 stress test. If you can only identify [3] and [4] in a controlled piano exercise with your eyes closed, that is Stage 2. Real-time identification in music you have not rehearsed is the gate.

After 6 minutes of active listening, run your 20-trial discrimination log. Your Week 4 target: 19-20/20.

---

### S2 — Integrated Work (18 min) — Week 4

**Objective this week:** Transpose. Everything you have done in Am, now try in Dm.

**Why transposition matters:** One of the five mastery criteria is "demonstrated in at least 3 keys." You have been living in Am. This week you enter Dm — a different minor key, same principles.

**Dm Zone 1:** D (root), F (third), A (fifth)
- On guitar: open 4th string = D (root); 2nd fret of 3rd string = A (fifth); 1st fret of 1st string = F (third). Play the open Dm chord to locate all three.
- On piano: D is two white keys above C. F is two white keys above D. A is four white keys above F (D-E-F-G-A). Play D-F-A as a three-note block chord.

**Exercise 1: Zone 1 in Dm (9 min)**

Repeat the Week 1 Zone 1 location exercise, but in Dm. Find D, F, A on both guitar and piano. Play D and F together — that is [3], the same minor-third quality you have been training all sprint, now starting from D instead of A. Play D and A together — that is [7] (same stable fifth). Play the full three-note Dm chord on both instruments.

Sing D-F-A ascending. Feel: same [3] quality from root to third. The minor quality is the same acoustic property regardless of what note it starts on.

**Exercise 2: Ain't No Sunshine-style loop in Dm (9 min)**

Create a simple Dm-C-Am loop (the equivalent of Am-G-Em in the key of D minor). Play it the same way you have been playing the Am loop — chord by chord, slow, tracking the harmony. Zone 1 improvise over it.

You will be slow and uncertain in Dm. This is correct. Transposing to a new key puts the skill back to Stage 2 temporarily. If it feels like starting over, it is supposed to feel that way. The question is: how quickly does it stabilize? If you can find Zone 1 in Dm within five minutes of exploration, you are genuinely close to Stage 3. If it takes the whole 9 minutes and you are still uncertain, you need more time in Am before attempting the gate.

---

### S3 — Song Work (14 min) — Week 4

**Objective this week:** Perform Ain't No Sunshine. Play it like someone is listening.

**The standard:** Start to finish, no stopping for mistakes, correct chords, recognizable rhythm, present with the music.

Play it once on guitar. All the way through. You will make mistakes — do not stop for them. A missed chord change, a buzzing string, a rhythm hiccup: all of these are survivable. Keep the pulse alive. Keep moving through the song.

Then play it once on piano. Same standard. Left hand roots, right hand Zone 1 melody or single melodic notes tracking the harmony.

After each pass, note what happened. Not what went wrong technically — but where did you feel the song? Was there a moment when the framework disappeared? Was there a moment when you stopped thinking and started listening? That moment — however brief — is the north star.

If there was no such moment either pass, play a third time through on whichever instrument felt closer. Chase the moment where the playing becomes music rather than execution.

---

### S4 — Free Play (10 min) — Week 4

**Objective this week:** No Am. No familiar key. Pick a different minor key and find Zone 1 from scratch.

Choose any note that is not A as your tonic. E minor, B minor, C minor, F minor — any of them. Do not choose Dm because you just worked in it during S2.

Find the root, third, and fifth of your chosen minor chord on one instrument. You should know the structure by now: the minor third is a [3] (three semitones) above the root, and the fifth is a [7] (seven semitones) above the root. Locate these by interval from your chosen root, not by memorizing note names.

Once you have found Zone 1 in your new key, improvise freely using only those three notes. No constraints on rhythm. Listen to the minor quality — [3] between root and third — and confirm that it sounds like minor. The same property you have been training all sprint, now expressed from a new starting point.

This is the furthest out from your comfort zone that Sprint 1 asks you to go. If it feels uncomfortable, stay with the discomfort. It is the right signal.

---

### S5 — The Synthesizer (8 min) — Week 4

**Objective this week:** The real synthesis. No scaffolding.

Do not play the Am loop. Do not play a chord you have rehearsed in this sprint. Pick up whichever instrument you feel most connected to today, and play music for 8 minutes.

The only constraint: use only what you own. Di-chords you can hear. Zone 1 notes you can locate. Rhythmic feel you have internalized. If you reach for something that is Stage 2 — uncertain, requiring thought — pull back to something Stage 3.

All four chambers running. Melody. Harmony. Voicings. Rhythm. Not separately, not in rotation — simultaneously, as they naturally exist in music.

No stopping. No evaluation during the 8 minutes. After: sit quietly for 30 seconds and notice what it felt like.

## Flexible Sprint Duration — When to Advance

Sprint 1 typically takes 3 to 5 weeks. Some students clear both gates in 3 weeks. Some take 6. Both are correct outcomes — not different outcomes. A student who takes 6 weeks because they genuinely own Stage 3 on everything is doing better than a student who moves in 3 weeks with Stage 2 work declared as Stage 3.

**The honest question:** Do not ask "have I done the exercises?" Ask "do I own the skill?"

**Self-check questions for the S1 Plogger gate:**

Ask these of yourself, not generously:
- If I am sitting in a coffee shop and a song comes on, can I hear whether the first interval of the melody is a [3] or [4]? Not after concentrating — immediately, automatically?
- If someone plays a minor third on a piano in the next room, do I recognize it? Or do I have to set up the context first?
- Can I sing a [3] from any pitch on demand, without a reference instrument?
- Can I draw the Pictograph for [3] and [4] from memory, including the three visual properties?

If all four are yes: you are at Stage 3 on the Plogger gate.

**Self-check questions for the performance gate:**

- Can I play Ain't No Sunshine on guitar, from start to finish, without stopping, in a way that sounds like the song — not just the correct notes?
- Can I play it on piano at the same standard?
- Can I sing the root of each chord while playing the other instrument?
- If someone plays a melody from the song and asks me "which Zone 1 note of Am is that?", can I answer without preparation?

If all four are yes: you are at Stage 3 on the performance gate.

**What to do if you are stuck:**

If [3]/[4] discrimination is plateaued at 14-16/20 after three weeks, the problem is almost certainly in the hearing step — you are naming before you have truly heard. The fix: extend the listening pause to ten seconds before naming. Do not rush. The nervous system needs time to register the sound as a distinct object.

If Ain't No Sunshine is still stopping on chord changes after three weeks, the problem is in the physical availability of the chord shapes — they are not ready before the change arrives. The fix: practice chord changes in isolation, without the song, switching between just two chords in a loop (Am to G, G to Em) until the change takes less than one beat to complete.

If both instruments feel fine separately but The Synthesizer falls apart, the problem is not synthesis failure — it is that one of the component skills is Stage 2 masquerading as Stage 3. Run the self-checks again, honestly.

## Sprint 1 Exit Assessment

The gate is two parts. Both must be cleared in the same session, without warm-up, to count. "I can do it when I practice" is Stage 2. The gate is Stage 3: without preparation, first attempt, at musical tempo.

---

### Gate B — Plogger Chapter 1-3 Mastery

**Administer this yourself, or have someone else run it.**

**Trial set 1 — Discrimination (10 trials):**

Have someone (or yourself, covering your eyes) play 10 intervals on piano: a random mix of [3] and [4], starting from different roots (not always A). After each interval plays, name it: "[3]" or "[4]." Required score: 10/10.

If administering to yourself: write down 10 choices (randomly — something like 3,4,3,3,4,4,3,4,4,3) before sitting at the piano. Then play what the list says and name it after hearing but before reading the list. Score yourself honestly.

**Trial set 2 — Singing [3] from 5 different pitches:**

Have someone call out (or yourself choose) five different pitches: for example, C, F#, Bb, D, G. From each pitch, sing the minor third above it — the [3]. Accuracy standard: you land within a half step of the correct pitch without using an instrument to check first. After singing, play the correct note on piano and confirm.

**Trial set 3 — Singing [4] from 5 different pitches:**

Same exercise, major third. Five different pitches. Sing [4] above each.

**Trial set 4 — Real-time identification (10 trials):**

A short melody is played on piano — five to eight notes. After each melodic step, identify whether the interval just played was [3] or [4] (or neither, in which case say "other"). Required: correctly identify at least 10 out of 10 trials that contain [3] or [4], while correctly passing on "other" intervals.

**Gate B passes when:** Trials 1 and 4 score 10/10. Trials 2 and 3 are accurate on 4 out of 5 pitches each.

### Performance Gate — Ain't No Sunshine

**Standard:** Simplified but musical. Not performance-ready by professional standard — but recognizable, continuous, and alive.

**Test 1 — Guitar:**

Play Ain't No Sunshine on guitar from start to finish:
- Correct chords (Am, G, Em or Dm) in the correct order
- A recognizable rhythmic feel (positions 1 and 5 minimum, with at least some variation)
- No complete stops — mistakes are survivable, but stopping the pulse is not
- All the way through, including the "I know" stop-time section

Pass standard: someone who knows the song would recognize it from your playing.

**Test 2 — Piano:**

Play Ain't No Sunshine on piano from start to finish:
- Left hand root notes on beat one (at minimum)
- Right hand Zone 1 notes or simplified melody tracking the harmony
- Correct chord changes in the correct order
- No complete stops

Pass standard: same as guitar — recognizable, continuous, alive.

**Test 3 — Root singing while playing:**

Play any chord from the song (guitar or piano — examiner or you choose). While playing it, sing the root note of the chord. Then change to the next chord and sing its root. The sung note must land on the correct pitch — within a half step. Do this for all three chords: Am (sing A), G (sing G), Em (sing E).

**Test 4 — Ear identification in the song:**

Someone plays a melody fragment from Ain't No Sunshine — any two-note move in the Am melody. Without preparation, identify which Zone 1 note it started on: root (A), third (C), or fifth (E) of Am. Required accuracy: 3 out of 4 fragments correctly identified.

**Sprint 1 is complete when:** Gate B passes AND all four performance tests pass, in the same session, without warm-up. On the same day.
