## CRITICAL RULES (re-read before every sprint build)

1. **ii-V-I is a DRILL VEHICLE, not the harmonic spine.** The full 12+2 progression set is the spine. Never let any sprint collapse into ii-V-I focus.
2. **Plogger appears TWICE per session:** isolated in Section 1 AND as perceptual lens woven through Sections 2–4.
3. **Hear/Sing Before You Play** is non-negotiable in every exercise across all sections.
4. **Few exercises, each multi-layered** — 2–3 exercises per section, each serving 3–4 purposes simultaneously.
5. **Anchor song threads every section** — it is not a separate activity.
6. **"The framework disappears"** is the north star. Everything points here.
7. **Mastery over pace** — sprint calendar is a guide, not a contract.
8. **No country** in repertoire or examples.
9. **Di-chord brackets always** — write [5] not "perfect fourth." Never use interval names alone without the bracket notation for di-chords. Exception: when using classical Roman numeral analysis, ordinal interval terms are acceptable in that context only.
10. **Label every exercise with its Stage (S1 / S2 / S3)**. Do not omit stage labels.

### How Progressions Relate to Di-Chord Movements

Progressions are not separate units to learn. They are **assemblies of movements you already own**. The student does not "learn the I–V–vi–IV progression" — they arrive at it naturally because they already own [7] (I→V), [3] (V→vi), and [9] (vi→IV) as separate sounds. By Sprint 8, a student who has genuinely mastered all seven movements can assemble any diatonic progression by ear. The 12+2 progressions become proof exercises, not lesson content.

### Non-Diatonic Callout Box Rule

Whenever a non-diatonic note or progression appears in any content, include a clearly marked callout box containing: (1) what it is, (2) why it sounds that way in di-chord terms, (3) how it connects to Heptachord Shift theory.

## DI-CHORD ROOT MOVEMENT SEQUENCE

One movement per sprint (Sprints 2–8). Sequenced by perceptual simplicity — most stable and most frequent intervals first.

| Sprint | Di-Chord | Semitone Count | Interval Name | Primary Root Movements | Perceptual Character | Learning Priority |
|--------|----------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 2 | [5] | 5 semitones | P4 up / P5 down | I→IV (opens), V→I (resolves) | The same interval — one reaches toward subdominant, one returns home. The resolution is [5]. | Highest — most stable, most frequent in all common-practice harmony |
| 3 | [7] | 7 semitones | P5 up / P4 down | I→V (the reach) | Dominant motion — the farthest stable reach before needing to return | High — inverse of [5], defines tonal polarity |
| 4 | [2] | 2 semitones | M2 up | IV→V (tension builder), V→vi (deceptive) | The smallest step up — tension that wants to release | High — smallest step, builds tension efficiently |
| 5 | [3] | 3 semitones | m3 down | I→vi (darkening) | The relative minor shift — same notes, different gravity center | Medium-High — quality shift without key change |
| 6 | [9] | 9 semitones | M6 up | vi→IV (emotional lift) | The inverse of [3] — brightness after minor | Medium — emotional color, inverse pair with [3] |
| 7 | [4] | 4 semitones | M3 up | I→iii (mediant) | The bittersweet third — neither fully major nor minor in feel | Medium — bittersweet quality, less frequent |
| 8 | [6] | 6 semitones | Tritone | V7→I (tritone resolution) | The most tense interval resolving — completes the full explanation of dominant function | Delayed — requires [5][7][2] as foundation first |

**Open Design Question (unresolved as of 2026-06-11):** The [5]/[7] split — I→IV uses [5] (P4 up) and V→I also uses [5] (P4 up, same di-chord). Sprint 2 currently holds both [5] movements on the premise they share the same di-chord. The alternative is to give V→I its own sprint because the resolution is so important. Not yet decided.

### Sprint 1 — Orientation (Fully Worked Example)

**Di-Chord Root Movement Focus:** None — this is the orientation sprint. No harmonic movement gate. Students are discovering and roughing in, not perfecting anything.

**Plogger Chapters:** Ch.1, Ch.2, Ch.3 (start)
- Ch.1: Three Stages of Learning (Stage 1 = Roughing In, Stage 2 = Perfecting, Stage 3 = Fluent). Every exercise labeled with its stage.
- Ch.2: Three Causes of Error (Reaction = use Eagle Vision; Anticipation = lead with correct sensory modality; Looking Back = "Coach out!"). Diagnose the cause before applying the cure.
- Ch.3 (start): Keyboard Visualization introduction. Note names, black-key landmark method, drawing exercises 3-1 through 3-5. Fixed-do solfège: doh=C, re=D, mi=E, fa=F, sol=G, la=A, si=B.

**Melody Content:**
- Zone: Zone 1 only (chord tones — 1̂, 3̂, 5̂). Stable, resolved, home.
- Major vs. minor quality by ear: [4] (major 3rd, modal, harmonic, open) vs. [3] (minor 3rd, modal, non-harmonic, closed). This is the foundational quality distinction.
- Gestural types: not yet introduced — save for Sprint 7+ (Ch.15).

**Harmony Content:**
- Progressions introduced: i–IV–i–V in A minor (from anchor song).
- Roman numerals: i (minor tonic), IV (major subdominant in harmonic minor context), V (major dominant).
- Focus is quality recognition, not progression theory. The harmonic minor raised 7̂ is mentioned but not analyzed deeply.

**Voicings Content:**
- Root position triads only — both instruments.
- Guitar: Am, Em, G, C, D, Dm, E7, F open chord shapes.
- Piano: A minor and C major conjunct heptachord formation (both hands). Di-chord to finger mapping: both index fingers = 1̂ (tonic), both middle fingers = 2̂/7̂ (dissonant), both ring fingers = 3̂/6̂ (modal), both pinkies = 4̂/5̂ (perfect).

**Rhythm Content:**
- Longy Rhythms: Ch.4 introduction. ta (quarter note), ta-te (two eighths). Speak numbers aloud, think through dots/rests. Never stop the pulse.
- No Rhythm Code or Lap Map yet — those begin Sprint 3.

**Anchor Song:** "Ain't No Sunshine" — Bill Withers, A minor
- Why: Minor quality (Zone 1 over minor chords), simple 3-chord structure, immediately accessible on both instruments, iconic groove, unambiguous minor feel reinforces [3]/[4] quality distinction.
- Key/Mode: A minor (Aeolian feel, harmonic minor inflection at V).

**Mastery Gates:**
- **Gate A (Harmonic):** Not applicable this sprint — no harmonic movement gate.
- **Gate B (Plogger):** All keyboard visualization landmarks named instantly by sight. Can draw keyboard from memory at accurate proportions. Three Causes of Error identified by symptom and correct cure named for each.

**Stage 3 Standard — What "Owned" Looks Like:**
- Hear any chord and instantly identify major vs. minor quality (no hesitation, no calculation).
- Name all white-key note names (solfège and letter) instantly at any keyboard position.
- Explain own practice errors using Three Causes vocabulary, not just "I made a mistake."
- Play "Ain't No Sunshine" chord progression on both instruments at musical tempo without stopping.

### Sprint 3 — [7] P5 Root Movement

**Harmony Content:**

## SPIRAL THREADS

Six skills that begin small and deepen through every sprint. They never complete. Progress is measured by increasing fluency and range, not by arrival at a final state.

### 1. Di-Chord Ear

**What it is:** The perceptual skill of recognizing any of the 11 di-chords ([1]–[11]) by ear as a distinct sound with its own character (pulsation, F/O factor, harmonicity). Later applied to harmonic root movements.

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 1–2: [3] vs. [4] only (major vs. minor quality)
- Sprint 3–4: [5] and [7] added (perfect di-chords)
- Sprint 5–6: Full [1]–[11] with all three sound factors
- Sprint 7–9: Di-chords applied to melodic gesture recognition
- Sprint 10–12: Di-chords in real-time harmonic analysis and improvisation

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Section 1 (Plogger Foundation) always contains a di-chord identification exercise. The Plogger chapter for that sprint determines which new di-chords are introduced. Previously introduced di-chords appear as review warm-ups. Label the exercise with the sprint's stage. Never let more than two sprints pass without explicitly practicing di-chord identification by ear.

### 2. Keyboard Visualization

**What it is:** A vivid mental image of the piano keyboard, used as a cognitive tracking surface for all musical analysis. "The thinking tool of the musician." Used for tracking, interval calculation, mode formation, and heptachord shift analysis.

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 1: Natural notes by landmark, drawing exercises, eyes-closed note naming
- Sprint 2–3: Chromatic notes, fixed-do solfège fluency, Pythagorean Ordering integrated
- Sprint 4–6: Intervals, di-chord calculation on the visual keyboard
- Sprint 7–9: Chords and triad profiles visualized, mode formation
- Sprint 10–12: All 8 clefs, transposition, silent practice anywhere

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Always specify that keyboard visualization exercises are done away from the physical keyboard once the sprint's stage warrants it. Never allow students to bypass visualization by using the physical keyboard as a crutch after Sprint 3. Every Tracking Page session uses the visualized keyboard as the working surface.

### 3. Longy Rhythms (Lap Map)

**What it is:** Two integrated rhythm systems. Longy = syllabic rhythm encoding (what the rhythm IS — notation level). Lap Map = beat hierarchy internalization in the body (groove level — does it FEEL right). The two together span notation → groove.

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 1–2: ta, ta-te; 2-beat and 3-beat blocks
- Sprint 3–4: Lap Map 2–4 beat patterns; 4-beat and 5-beat Longy blocks
- Sprint 5–6: Lap Map through 6-beat; Son Clave integration
- Sprint 7–9: Lap Map through 7-beat; clave fluency both orientations
- Sprint 10–12: Full chart fluency; Longy + Rhythm Code fully integrated

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Section 1 (Plogger) gets 2–3 minutes of Longy Chart work every session. Section 4 (Jamming) begins with 2 minutes of Lap Map at q=120 minimum before any melodic work. Never allow Lap Map practice below q=120 — tempo that is too slow inhibits groove formation. Specify block and beat pattern for each sprint.

### 4. Melody Zones

**What it is:** A scale-degree stability ladder (Zone 1 = chord tones, Zone 2 = mild tension, Zone 3 = active tension, Zone 4 = wildcard) that gives the student a perceptual framework for melody analysis and composition. Maps directly to di-chord harmonicity.

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 1: Zone 1 only (chord tones, stable)
- Sprint 2: Zone 2 added (7̂ and 2̂, mild tension)
- Sprint 3: 5 Key Notes + Tiny Tension Arc
- Sprint 4: Duration Amplifies Zone
- Sprint 5: Zone 3 and Zone Distribution Ratios by section
- Sprint 6: Zone 4 (LA, Emotional Wildcard)
- Sprint 7–9: Chord-change behaviors deepened, gestural vocabulary active
- Sprint 10–12: 8-Step Melody Audit in real time, full improvisation zone fluency

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Section 3 (Song Learning) always contains a zone analysis of the anchor song melody. Early sprints (1–4) label zones explicitly for student. Later sprints (7+) require student to identify zones without labeling provided. The zone system connects directly to Plogger di-chord harmonicity — whenever zone work appears, note the di-chord connection.

### 5. Rhythm Code Groove

**What it is:** A 16-position guide (2 measures × 8 positions per measure in 4/4) that maps where melody and bass notes should land for groove, where they work with context, and where they clash unless intentional. Rhythm = starting points, not duration values.

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 3: Binary Grid introduced (8 positions per measure)
- Sprint 4: Stops and anticipations
- Sprint 5: Son Clave introduced as 5-note groove reference
- Sprint 6: 4-Step Groove Development (start boring → add anticipations → add stops → sync with clave)
- Sprint 7–9: Groove map applied to all active progressions
- Sprint 10–12: Groove vocabulary complete; Rhythm Code + Longy fully integrated; groove as unconscious background

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Section 4 (Jamming/Free Play) is the primary home for Rhythm Code work. Give specific starting prompts: "Start with quarter notes only over [progression]. Add one anticipation. Add one stop." Never assign free improv without a rhythmic constraint — the constraint is the learning. By Sprint 9+, the clave orientation should be specified in each jamming prompt.

### 6. Lap Map (Physical Keyboard / Silent Practice)

**What it is:** The physical beat-hierarchy diagram (thighs as a map for strong/weak beats) used both for rhythm internalization AND, in its secondary application, as a portable physical keyboard for silent practice anywhere. Left leg = strong beats, right leg = weak beats. Darkness gradient = dynamic map (hip = loudest, knee = quietest).

**Note:** The primary purpose is rhythm internalization. The keyboard-mapping secondary application begins only after rhythm use is established (Sprint 3+).

**Deepens sprint-to-sprint:**
- Sprint 3: Beat hierarchy introduction (2-beat, 3-beat, 4-beat)
- Sprint 4–5: 5-beat and 6-beat patterns; three-element integration (auditory + kinesthetic + visual)
- Sprint 6–8: 7-beat patterns; all groupings (3+2+2, 2+2+3, 2+3+2)
- Sprint 9–12: Tetrachord and conjunct heptachord formation practiced on lap map during idle time (waiting, commuting); silent mode modulation practice

**How an agent should handle it in sprint documents:** Always specify which lap map pattern applies to the current sprint's time signature. Section 1 (Plogger Foundation) includes 2–3 minutes of lap map groove before any pitch work. The "silent practice anywhere" application should be mentioned as a sidebar instruction starting Sprint 9, with specific lap-map exercises for off-keyboard practice.

## THE TWO PRODUCTS PER SPRINT

Every sprint produces two documents. Both are required. An agent must not write only one.

### Product 1: Mini-Textbook (Knowledge + Theory + WHY)

**Purpose:** Give the student everything they need to understand what they are learning and why. This is the conceptual + theoretical document. The student reads this before touching an instrument.

**Structure:**
1. **Sprint Overview** — What this sprint is about, what movements are being learned, what harmonic territory is covered. One paragraph.
2. **The Week's Music Theory** — The harmonic and melodic theory for this sprint, explained using di-chord vocabulary. No interval names without brackets. Roman numeral notation where applicable.
3. **Plogger Chapter Summary** — Plain-language explanation of what the Plogger chapters cover this sprint. What concepts the student will build. Why these concepts matter.
4. **Anchor Song Analysis** — Annotated lead sheet or chord chart showing where the sprint's target movement appears. Zone labeling over the melody. Named di-chords in the bass movement. Stage-appropriate depth.
5. **What Owned Looks Like** — The Stage 3 standard for this sprint, written in plain language from the student's first-person perspective ("You will know you own this when you can..."). Must reference all five mastery criteria.
6. **Non-Diatonic Callout Boxes** — If any non-diatonic notes or progressions appear this sprint, each gets its own callout box.
7. **Cross-Chamber Connections** — A brief section connecting this sprint's theory to at least two other chambers. (Example: "The [5] movement you're hearing in the bass is the same interval that gives Zone 1 chord tones their stability — [0], [4], [7] are all related to the perfect/harmonic di-chords.")

**Page targets:**
- Sprints 1–4: 8–12 pages (orientation and foundation content is denser)
- Sprints 5–8: 6–10 pages
- Sprints 9–12: 6–9 pages

**HTML-first:** Write in HTML with semantic structure. PDF is generated from HTML. Do not write for PDF layout — write for clear semantic HTML that will render correctly in a web view.

**Required callout box types:** Non-Diatonic Note (blue border), Stage 3 Standard (green border), Cross-Chamber Connection (gold border), Critical Reminder (red border).

### Product 2: Practice Plan + Workbook (HOW to Practice)

**Purpose:** Tell the student exactly what to do in each practice session, for how long, in what order. This is the operational document. The student refers to this during practice.

**Structure — Five Practice Sections (both instruments, every session):**

| Section | Time | Purpose | Design Constraint |
|---------|------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 — Plogger Foundation | ~12 min | Dedicated, isolated Plogger chapter work | Exact chapter and exercises specified. Stage labeled. Di-chord ear work included. |
| 2 — Integrated Work | ~20 min | 2–3 multi-layered exercises | Each exercise serves 3–4 purposes simultaneously. Explicit Hear/Sing Before You Play instruction. Lap Map and/or Longy integrated. |
| 3 — Song Learning/Practice | ~15 min | Anchor song — learn, analyze, memorize, play at tempo | Zone analysis embedded. Target root movement found in song. Specific measure references. |
| 4 — Jamming/Free Play | ~13 min | Structured improv prompts + creative exploration | Rhythmic constraint always specified. Clave orientation specified Sprint 5+. Stage labeled. |

**Session design rule:** Sections 2–4 always reference the sprint's anchor song. The song is the thread connecting all sections. Do not treat Section 3 as the only "song section."

**Workbook pages (included at end of each practice plan):**
- Self-assessment grid: Stage 1/2/3 rating for each exercise in each section
- Zone analysis worksheet (blank measures with instruction to map zones over anchor song melody)
- Di-chord tracking page (blank staff lines for student to write secondary and primary di-chords for a short excerpt)
- Progress checkpoint: "Check Gate A" and "Check Gate B" with specific observable criteria

**Exit Assessment Format:**
- **Gate A check:** One specific observable test the student performs. Written as: "Play [specific task] from [starting note(s)]. You pass if: [observable criteria]. You do not pass if: [observable failure mode — be specific]."
- **Gate B check:** Same format for Plogger chapter work.
- **Stage 3 self-assessment:** One sentence the student reads aloud: "I own this sprint when [Stage 3 standard]."
- **Advancement decision:** Explicit instruction — "If both gates are clear, advance to Sprint [N+1]. If either gate is not clear, repeat [specific section/exercise] before advancing."

### Notation Style Guide

1. **Di-chord brackets always:** Write [5], not "perfect fourth" alone. When using ordinal names in classical analysis context, include the bracket: "a perfect fourth ([5]) in the bass." Never use ordinal names as the primary identification — always anchor to the di-chord number.

2. **Roman numerals:** Uppercase for major (I, IV, V), lowercase for minor (ii, iii, vi), lowercase + ° for diminished (vii°). This is Plogger Ch.23 convention.

3. **Scale degrees with hat:** Always 1̂, 2̂, 3̂, 4̂, 5̂, 6̂, 7̂ — not "the first scale degree" or "scale degree 1."

4. **Stage labels on every exercise:** S1, S2, or S3 in a small badge or inline label. Never omit.

5. **Mastery criteria as observable criteria:** Write gate tests as specific, observable behaviors — not vague goals. "Play this at 80 BPM from memory" not "know this well."

6. **Solfège notation:** Fixed-do only. doh=C, re=D, mi=E, fa=F, sol=G, la=A, si=B. Never moveable-do.

7. **No interval names alone:** "A perfect 5th" without "[7]" is incomplete notation. Exception: when quoting directly from Plogger source text, preserve original terminology but add bracket notation in parentheses.

8. **Hear/Sing Before You Play label:** Every integrated exercise gets this instruction, either as a callout or as the first line. Non-negotiable. Never omit it as "implied."

9. **Progression numbers:** Use #1 through #14 to reference progressions, or state the full Roman numeral sequence. "Progression 4 (ii–V–I)" not just "ii–V–I" in isolation.
