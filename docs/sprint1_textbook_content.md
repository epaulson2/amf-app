## Introduction: What This Sprint Is About

Sprint 1 has no harmonic gate. That is its defining feature, and understanding why that is the case is the first thing you need to understand about this entire system.

Every other sprint in the AMF asks you to own something specific — a di-chord root movement, heard and playable by ear in three or more keys, at musical tempo, on both instruments, without hesitation. That is the gate. Sprint 1 does not set that gate because you are not ready to use it yet. Not because the material is beneath you — you know what a chord is, you know what an interval is — but because you do not yet have the perceptual language to build the habit in the right direction. Sprint 1's job is to establish that language.

What "no harmonic gate" means in practice is this: Sprint 1 ends when your ears have opened to the di-chord system at its most fundamental level. Specifically, when you can reliably distinguish [3] from [4] — the minor third from the major third — by sound alone, without an instrument, without notation, just by listening. That is the Sprint 1 gate, and it is a real gate. It sounds simple. It is not trivial. Reaching Stage 3 on this distinction — always correct at tempo without conscious attention — is a genuine achievement for an adult brain that has spent decades hearing music without this vocabulary.

The system you are entering is called the Plogger Method, and it forms the Musical OS of the AMF — the perceptual infrastructure that everything else builds on. The Plogger Method is not music theory in the conventional sense. It is not a system for reading notation, or for knowing chord names, or for analyzing harmonic progressions. It is a system for training your conscious mind to hear what your ears are already perceiving. That distinction matters enormously. Plogger wrote: "The ear needs no training. It is the mind that requires training to become aware of what the ear is hearing at the speed that the ear is hearing it." Sprint 1 is the beginning of that training.

The anchor song for this sprint is "Ain't No Sunshine" by Bill Withers, recorded in 1971. It was chosen for three reasons that line up precisely with what Sprint 1 needs to establish. First, it is in A minor — which means you will be living inside a minor sound world for the entire sprint, and that minor quality is exactly what [3] versus [4] is all about. Second, it is a three-chord song with a simple, clear harmonic structure that will let you focus your attention on sound quality rather than navigating complexity. Third, it grooves in a way that makes you want to play it again. This matters more than it sounds — you will play this song dozens of times before the sprint is done, and it needs to be worth that.

By the end of Sprint 1, you will be able to do five things that you cannot fully do right now. You will be able to hear a major third and a minor third and know immediately which is which. You will be able to locate Zone 1 — the chord tones — of any chord on both instruments. You will understand the difference between a chord's name and a chord's function. You will have a working feel for the 8-position rhythmic grid and what it means for a song to groove. And you will be able to play "Ain't No Sunshine" in a simplified but musical form on both guitar and piano. Not perfectly. Musically.

That is what this sprint is for.

## Part 1: The Musical OS — How Plogger Works

### What a Di-Chord Is

The Plogger Method begins with a decision about what to count. Traditional music theory counts intervals ordinally: a second, a third, a fourth. The ordinal system tells you the rank — the position — of the upper note relative to the lower note within a diatonic scale. So a "third" means the upper note is the third degree of a scale starting from the lower note. The problem with this for perceptual training is that ordinal position does not correspond to how the interval sounds. A "third" on the staff can be two different sounds depending on whether it is three or four semitones wide. The staff hides this. Sheet music gives you ordinal information — position — and asks you to infer everything else.

The di-chord system starts over. A di-chord number is a cardinal count of semitones, written in square brackets: [1], [2], [3], through [11]. There are exactly 11 audible interval classes within the octave — not twelve, because the octave itself ([12]) sounds like a return to the same note, not a new di-chord. Each bracket number tells you something acoustically specific. It tells you how many semitones apart the two notes are, which determines their interference pulsation rate, which in turn determines a large part of their perceptual character.

The bracket notation system directly solves the problem that the staff creates. [3] is always the minor third — three semitones — regardless of what key you are in, regardless of clef, regardless of whether you are reading a treble or bass staff. [4] is always the major third — four semitones. The system is absolute. It describes the physics of what two pitches produce when they sound together, not the notation convention used to represent them.

This shift from ordinal to cardinal — from "position in the scale" to "count of semitones" — is not just a different number system. It is a different question. Traditional interval naming asks: where does this note sit in the scale? The di-chord system asks: what do these two notes do to each other when they sound? That is a physical question, and it has a physical answer. The answer is pulsation.

The inversion law follows directly from the cardinal system. Any di-chord added to its inversion equals twelve, because one octave equals twelve semitones. [3] + [9] = 12. [5] + [7] = 12. [4] + [8] = 12. This is not a memorization trick — it is the arithmetic of the octave. Knowing it means that once you own [3] and [4] as sounds, you already have a preliminary relationship to [9] and [8] by inversion, even before you study them directly.

The full table of di-chords, with their traditional names alongside for orientation:

| Di-Chord | Semitones | Traditional Name |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| [1] | 1 | minor 2nd |
| [2] | 2 | major 2nd |
| [3] | 3 | minor 3rd |
| [4] | 4 | major 3rd |
| [5] | 5 | perfect 4th |
| [6] | 6 | tritone (aug 4th / dim 5th) |
| [7] | 7 | perfect 5th |
| [8] | 8 | minor 6th |
| [9] | 9 | major 6th |
| [10] | 10 | minor 7th |
| [11] | 11 | major 7th |

Eleven sounds. Each one a distinct perceptual character. Not positions on a page — sounds in the world.

The reason the Plogger Method uses the keyboard as its cognitive tool is precisely because the keyboard makes these eleven distances visible. The piano keyboard shows twelve semitones in one octave, repeated across the full instrument. It is the one instrument where semitone count is directly visible as physical distance. When you can visualize a keyboard clearly and fluently, you can count di-chords in your mind without touching an instrument. That mental keyboard is the working surface on which all Plogger exercises run.

### The Three Sound Factors

A di-chord is not one-dimensional. Saying that [3] is a minor third tells you the semitone count — that is the first dimension. But it does not tell you how the interval feels, what its acoustic behavior is, or how to distinguish it from other intervals in the same neighborhood. For that you need three sound factors, each of which addresses a different physical property of what two simultaneously sounding notes do.

The three factors are: Interference Pulsation, the F/O Factor, and Harmonicity. Together they give every di-chord a unique identity — no two di-chords share the same combination of all three. This means that if you hold all three factors simultaneously in your awareness, you can identify any di-chord. If you ignore even one of them, you will sometimes confuse di-chords that share the other two.

**Interference Pulsation**

When two notes of slightly different frequencies sound together, the pressure waves interfere with each other and create a rhythmic beating. You have heard this when a poorly tuned guitar string beats against a reference pitch — that wobbling, pulsing quality is acoustic interference. In di-chords this beating is inherent to the interval itself, not a sign of bad tuning. Two notes a minor third apart will always beat at a specific rate, regardless of how precisely they are tuned within that interval class.

Plogger identifies three pulsation categories based on rate:

**Dissonant (8Hz):** Fast, urgent beating. The wave shape is jagged. Di-chords [1], [2], [10], and [11] all fall here — the minor and major seconds and their inversions. These are what traditional theory calls "seconds and sevenths." At 8Hz you are hearing the interference wave cycle roughly eight times per second, which is fast enough to create an urgent, restless quality. These di-chords are not comfortable to sit in. They demand movement.

**Modal (4Hz):** Smooth, rounded beating. Sometimes described as "wobbly." Di-chords [3], [4], [8], and [9] — the minor and major thirds and their inversions, the sixths. The wave shape in the Pictograph is rounded, flowing. Four cycles per second creates a quality that is neither urgent nor stable — it is emotionally active, which is why "modal" is the right word for it. These are the di-chords of feeling.

**Perfect (2Hz):** Slow, flat, almost imperceptible beating. Di-chords [5], [6], and [7] — the perfect fourth, tritone, and perfect fifth. Two cycles per second is so slow that the beating almost disappears into a sense of stillness. These di-chords feel suspended, stable, or frozen depending on their other properties.

The pulsation pattern across the eleven di-chords is palindromic: Dissonant–Dissonant–Modal–Modal–Perfect–Perfect–Perfect–Modal–Modal–Dissonant–Dissonant. The system is symmetric around [6]. This symmetry is not a coincidence — it reflects the physical symmetry of the overtone series.

**F/O Factor (Shadow Quality)**

The F/O Factor describes the gravitational direction of a di-chord: does the upper note refer downward toward the lower note (the Fundamental), or upward toward the octave of the lower note? This is not about which note is "more important" — it is about acoustic gravity, about where the di-chord's center of weight lies.

The rule is simple: small intervals gravitate toward the fundamental, large intervals reach toward the octave.

Di-chords [1] through [5] — the lower half of the system — all refer down. The upper note gravitates toward the lower note's fundamental. These di-chords are anchored, terrestrial. Di-chords [7] through [11] — the upper half — all refer up. The upper note reaches toward the octave of the lower note. These di-chords are expansive, sky-reaching. [6], the tritone exactly in the middle, splits the octave in half and refers both directions simultaneously, which is why it has a uniquely ambiguous, destabilized quality.

In the Pictograph system, this factor appears as shadow direction: a shadow cast to the left means the di-chord refers down (toward the fundamental); a shadow cast to the right means it refers up (toward the octave); [6] has shadows on both sides.

The F/O factor is what lets you distinguish between di-chords with similar pulsation rates. [3] and [8] are both modal (4Hz, rounded wave shape), but [3] has a left shadow (refers down, fundamentally anchored) and [8] has a right shadow (refers up, octave-reaching). They feel different not because of their pulsation — that is the same — but because of their gravitational direction.

**Harmonicity (Color Quality)**

Harmonicity describes whether the upper note of a di-chord belongs to the overtone series of the lower note. This is a question about acoustic kinship. When any note sounds, it does not produce just one frequency — it produces a stack of overtone partials above the fundamental. The first nine partials of this overtone series are the notes most closely related to the root. If the upper note of a di-chord falls within those first nine partials, the di-chord is harmonic. If it falls outside them, it is non-harmonic.

The perceptual result of this physics is what Plogger describes as color:

**Harmonic di-chords** ([2], [4], [7], [9], [10]) sound open, expanding, alkaline. The two notes blend together because the upper note already "belongs to" the lower note's natural sound. It is like a green ornament on a green Christmas tree — it fits in. The notes seem to repel each other slightly, like magnets of the same polarity, which gives harmonic di-chords an airy, spreading quality.

**Non-harmonic di-chords** ([1], [3], [5], [8], [11]) sound closed, contracting, acidic. The upper note stands apart from the lower note's overtone family. A red ornament on a green tree — it stands out. The notes attract each other, like opposite-polarity magnets, which gives non-harmonic di-chords a compressed, dense quality.

**[6]** is exceptional in all three factors. Its pulsation is perfect (2Hz) but it produces a frozen, ambiguous quality rather than the stable open feeling of [7]. Its F/O factor is both. Its harmonicity is neutral — neither harmonic nor non-harmonic. [6] is the outlier of the system, which explains why the tritone has always been theoretically special: it defies the normal patterns.

**[9]** is a special case called false harmonic. It sounds like a harmonic di-chord — open, warm, upward-reaching — but technically the upper note does not fall within the first nine partials of the lower note's overtone series. It is perceived as harmonic but is technically non-harmonic. This is why Plogger uses the term "false harmonic" — the perception and the physics are misaligned.

Here is the complete sound factor table for all eleven di-chords, drawn directly from the AMF database:

|----------|-----------|------|------|------------|-------------|-----------------|

This table is not something to memorize as a list. It is a description of sounds you will learn to recognize by ear, one di-chord at a time. Sprint 1 gives you two of them — [3] and [4]. Every subsequent sprint adds one more. By Sprint 8 you own all eleven, and the table becomes a reference for a vocabulary you already speak.

### Sprint 1 Focus: [3] and [4] — The Quality Di-Chords

Sprint 1 begins with [3] and [4] because these two di-chords encode the most fundamental distinction in Western music: minor versus major. When you hear a chord and feel that it is minor — that specific quality of weight and inwardness — what you are hearing is a minor third sitting inside the chord. When you hear a chord as major — that open, bright, resolved quality — you are hearing a major third. The quality question is a [3] versus [4] question. Everything else in harmony builds from this.

Let us look at their sound factors precisely, because this is where the difference between "I sort of know the difference" and "I own this" becomes clear.

[3] — the minor third — is modal (4Hz, rounded wave), refers down toward the fundamental, and is non-harmonic (dark). The non-harmonic quality means the upper note stands apart from the lower note's overtone family. The notes attract each other. Combined with the 4Hz modal beating, [3] has a quality that is both flowing (modal) and slightly closed (non-harmonic, fundamental-referring). This is the sound of minor — not merely sad or dark as a mood label, but perceptually contracted and inward. Acoustically, the two notes of a minor third want to converge.

[4] — the major third — is also modal (4Hz, rounded wave) and also refers down. It shares [3]'s pulsation and shadow direction exactly. The only difference is harmonicity: [4] is harmonic (light). The upper note falls within the first nine partials of the lower note's overtone series. The notes repel slightly — open, expanding. This is the sound of major: the same flowing, emotional quality of [3], but acoustically open rather than closed.

This comparison exposes why learning to hear these two di-chords is the right starting point. They are maximally similar in two out of three factors — same pulsation, same F/O direction — and differ only in harmonicity. To reliably distinguish [3] from [4] you have to be attending to color, to the closed versus open quality. That is an act of perceptual precision that trains the exact listening faculty the entire Plogger system requires.

The Hear → Sing → Name workflow is the method. It runs in this exact sequence, always:

First, you hear the di-chord. You play [3] or [4] and you simply listen. Not to analyze. Not to name. Just to receive the sound. This stage is often rushed, and the rushing destroys the learning. You need to sit inside the sound long enough for your nervous system to register it as an object — as a thing with a character, not just a pair of notes. Five seconds. Ten seconds. Longer if you need it.

Then you sing it. Singing forces your body to produce the interval, not just perceive it. The act of singing a [3] from any starting pitch requires that your vocal cords, breath, and muscular system all encode the interval. You cannot fake this the way you can fake hearing. If you can sing [3] from any starting note on demand — if you can produce it without reference to an instrument — then something real has happened in your nervous system.

Then you name it. At this point naming is not difficult because you have already heard and produced the interval. The name is just the label for something you already know. This is the opposite of traditional ear training, which often starts with names and tries to work backward to sounds. Plogger's sequence is designed to ensure that the ear leads and the label follows — never the reverse.

The goal state for Sprint 1 is this: you hear two notes — any two notes — and you know within a second or two whether they are a [3] or a [4]. Not because you have counted semitones or analyzed the notation. Because you have heard it enough times that the sound itself has become recognizable, the way a familiar voice is recognizable without looking. The difference between [3] and [4] becomes as unambiguous as the difference between a knock and a tap. This is what Stage 3 feels like on this distinction. It is entirely achievable in a single sprint for an adult who practices with real attention.

### The Pictograph System

The Plogger Pictograph is a visual encoding of all three sound factors for each di-chord, drawn as a stylized rendering of the bracket number itself. Each di-chord has a visual glyph that encodes its pulsation, F/O factor, and harmonicity simultaneously, using three visual properties:

**Shape/outline** encodes pulsation. Dissonant di-chords ([1], [2], [10], [11]) are drawn with jagged, angular outlines — the visual equivalent of fast, urgent beating. Modal di-chords ([3], [4], [8], [9]) have smooth, rounded, flowing outlines. Perfect di-chords ([5], [6], [7]) have straight edges.

**Shadow direction** encodes the F/O factor. Di-chords that refer down toward the fundamental ([1] through [5]) have their shadow cast to the left. Di-chords that refer up toward the octave ([7] through [11]) have their shadow cast to the right. [6], which refers both directions, has shadows on both sides.

**Color shade** encodes harmonicity. Harmonic di-chords are rendered in light — open, bright. Non-harmonic di-chords are rendered in dark — dense, closed. [6] is neutral.

What the Pictograph system does is give you an additional cognitive channel for retaining and distinguishing di-chords. When you visualize the pictograph of [3] — a rounded shape, shadow left, dark color — you are simultaneously rehearsing all three of its sound factors. The visual acts as a mnemonic that encodes the acoustic properties you are training to hear.

The relationship between the visual and the perceptual is not arbitrary. A rounded dark shape with a left shadow really does capture something true about the minor third — its smooth flowing quality, its heaviness, its gravitational pull downward. A rounded light shape with a left shadow captures something true about the major third — same flow, but brighter, more open. The Pictograph is not a cute icon system. It is a cross-modal encoding of genuine acoustical properties.

In practice, when working on Plogger exercises, you visualize the Pictograph before singing or playing a di-chord. This activates the visual-spatial memory of the interval alongside the auditory and motor memory. The three channels reinforce each other.

Exercises in Chapters 11, 12, and 13 build the Pictograph progressively: first you draw the pulsation outlines alone, then you add shadow direction, then you add color. By Chapter 14 you can draw the complete Pictograph for all eleven di-chords from memory. The drawing is not an art project — it is a fluency check. If you can draw it, you own it.

For Sprint 1, the Pictograph work focuses on [3] and [4]. You learn their shapes, shadows, and colors. You draw them. You visualize them before each hearing exercise. You are building the mental representation that will anchor these two sounds in long-term memory.

### The Tracking Page

The Tracking Page is one of the two most important tools in the entire Plogger Method. Plogger himself writes that "next to visualizing the keyboard, the Tracking Page is the most important means of developing fluency and accuracy in connecting your mind with your ear and your eye."

It is a specific page in the Plogger book — page 153 — containing ten melodic lines built around specific di-chord types. No clefs are printed on these lines. You choose a starting note each day, which determines the mode. If you start on the note C (doh in fixed solfège), the mode is Ionian. If you start on A (la), the mode is Aeolian. Seven different starting notes, seven different modes, the same ten melodic lines. One starting note per day of the week.

Each line is practiced through six protocols, in order. Protocol 1: speak the solfège name of each note. Protocol 2: speak the secondary di-chord number — the di-chord between consecutive notes. Protocol 3: speak the primary di-chord number — the di-chord between each note and the tonic. Protocol 4: sing the primary di-chord number while playing a drone at the keyboard. Protocol 5A: sing the secondary di-chord number while visualizing its Pictograph. Protocol 6: sing the solfège names.

The full six protocols are not all introduced at once. In Sprint 1, you begin with Protocols 1 and 2. The deeper protocols arrive as your di-chord ear develops through later sprints. By the time you reach Protocol 6 in Sprint 12, you are working the Tracking Page with all six protocols in seven modes simultaneously — and the result is a level of melodic perception that ordinary music theory study does not produce.

The reason the Tracking Page works is captured in Plogger's distinction between reading by rote and reading by di-chord. Reading by rote means identifying individual notes one at a time — pushing the right buttons. Reading by di-chord means accessing the relationships between notes, the intervals of movement, "where expression, affect, and meaning are found." The Tracking Page trains the latter.

For Sprint 1, the Tracking Page serves as a commitment device more than a full practice protocol. You are establishing the habit of noticing di-chords in music you encounter outside your practice session. When you hear a melody in a song, in a film, in an advertisement — you begin asking: what is the quality of that interval? Is it major-third or minor-third? That ongoing noticing is the beginning of the real ear training. The Tracking Page structures and records it.

## Part 2: Melody — Zone 1

### What the Zone System Is

The Melody Chamber of the AMF organizes melodic notes by what they do in relation to the underlying harmony, not by their pitch names or their position in a scale. This is a functional view of melody: every note in a melody is either at home, slightly away from home, significantly away from home, or approaching from outside. The zone system makes this gradient explicit.

The logic behind organizing melody this way comes from a simple question about any given note in a melodic phrase: how stable is this note over the chord that is currently sounding? A note that belongs to the chord — that is part of the chord's own construction — is maximally stable. A note that does not belong to the chord but sits close to a chord tone is moderately unstable. A note that sits a half step away from a chord tone, creating maximum friction, is quite unstable. A note that approaches the chord from outside the scale entirely is the most volatile.

Tension and release in melody is almost entirely a function of these stability levels. When a melody feels like it is pulling somewhere, leaning toward something, aching to resolve — that is the ear responding to instability zones. When a melody feels settled, landed, finished — that is Zone 1 at work.

The four zones are:

**Zone 1:** Chord tones — the root, third, and fifth of the current chord. These are the notes from which the chord is built. They are maximally stable over that chord because they belong to it.

**Zone 2:** Non-chord tones — the 7th scale degree and the 2nd scale degree (relative to the tonic). These notes are one diatonic step away from chord tones. They create mild tension but are close enough to home that they feel like passing through, not arriving somewhere uncomfortable.

**Zone 3:** Color tones — specifically the fourth scale degree, which creates what traditional theory calls a suspended or "plagal" quality. This note stands directly in conflict with the third of the chord. It creates active, clear tension that wants to resolve.

**Zone 4:** Approach tones — chromatic notes, notes from outside the scale, that are used to approach chord tones from a half step above or below.

The zones are a spiral thread, which means Zone 1 enters in Sprint 1 and the system deepens sprint by sprint. Zone 2 enters in Sprint 3. Zone 3 in Sprint 5. Zone 4 in Sprint 7. You do not learn all four zones in Sprint 1 — you establish Zone 1 as a complete, fluent skill before Zone 2 is introduced.

### Zone 1 in Detail

Zone 1 contains exactly three notes for any given chord: the root, the third, and the fifth. These are the building blocks of a triad, and they carry specific functions that are worth understanding in depth rather than just memorizing.

**The Root** is home. When a melody lands on the root of the current chord, it is as resolved as it can be. The root is tonic gravity at the melodic level — the note that says "we are here." Staying on the root provides maximum rest. Moving away from it creates forward motion. In a minor chord, the root is the center of that particular gravity — the note that anchors the minor sound.

**The Third** is quality. The third of a chord is what determines whether the chord sounds major or minor — whether its quality is [4] (major, harmonic, open) or [3] (minor, non-harmonic, closed). This means that a melody note on the third of a chord is specifically enacting the quality of that chord. When Bill Withers sings the minor third of Am, he is not just choosing a stable note — he is voicing the chord's minor quality through the melody. The third is the most emotionally specific Zone 1 note.

**The Fifth** provides stability without direction. The fifth of a chord — which in a triad forms a perfect fifth above the root, a [7] di-chord — is stable (the [7] di-chord is the most open, harmonic, grounded of all di-chords), but it does not carry the same tonal gravity as the root. The fifth does not pull you home the way the root does, and it does not color the harmony the way the third does. It sits in the chord calmly, without urgency in either direction. In melody, the fifth functions as a place to rest that does not feel finished — comfortable but open.

Applied to "Ain't No Sunshine": the song moves primarily among three chords — Am (A-C-E), G (G-B-D), and Em (E-G-B), with some arrangements using Dm (D-F-A) in place of Em for the second chord slot. The Zone 1 notes for each chord are:

| Chord | Root | Third | Fifth |
|-------|------|-------|-------|
| Am | A | C | E |
| G | G | B | D |
| Em | E | G | B |
| Dm | D | F | A |

On guitar, these notes are present in every open voicing of these chords, distributed across the strings. The Am chord in open position gives you A on the open fifth string and at the second fret of the third string, C at the first fret of the second string, and E on the open first and fourth strings. Every string you strum in that chord voicing is Zone 1.

On piano, Zone 1 for Am is visible as a physical shape on the keys: A-C-E, a span of a fifth with C sitting inside it. The minor quality is literally visible in the distance from A to C (three semitones, [3], dark, non-harmonic) versus from A to E (seven semitones, [7], light, harmonic, stable). You can see [3] and [7] in the shape of the chord on the keys.

Finding Zone 1 by ear — rather than by memorizing note names — is the real skill. The workflow is: hear the chord, feel where the chord sounds like home, locate those notes under your fingers. Root, third, fifth are not abstract labels. They are acoustic landing zones that your ear can identify if you listen to how stable different notes feel over the current chord.

### Melodic Gesture Recognition

A melodic gesture is a shaped movement in melody — two notes connected by a specific di-chord interval, carrying a direction and a character. [3] ascending has a different gesture quality than [3] descending. [7] ascending has a quality Plogger calls "open." [3] descending is "closed." These are not metaphors — they are descriptions of the F/O factor made audible at the melodic level.

Sprint 1 is exposure to the concept of melodic gesture, not mastery of it. The full gesture vocabulary is a later sprint project, beginning with the Tracking Page's deep protocols in Sprint 7. What you are doing in Sprint 1 is priming your ear to notice that melody moves by intervals — that a step up from E to G in the Am context is a [3] gesture, and that it has a quality you can hear and eventually name.

The most immediate access to melodic gesture in Sprint 1 is through singing. When you sing the melody of "Ain't No Sunshine," you are producing melodic gestures with your voice. The up-step from the root to the third of Am — from A to C — is a [3]. The fall from E back down to A — a [5] — gives a suspended, hanging quality. These are gestures. They live in the melody as shapes, not just as note sequences.

### Pitch Before Rhythm

The AMF sequences pitch location before rhythmic complexity for a specific reason. Before you can feel the rhythm of a melody fully, you need to know where the notes live in musical space. If you are spending cognitive attention locating pitches — figuring out where C is on the fretboard, or where F is on the piano — you cannot also feel the time and groove that the rhythm requires. These are competing cognitive loads.

By establishing Zone 1 fluency — by getting to the point where you can locate A, C, and E over Am without conscious searching — you free up attention for rhythm. The hands go to the right notes without deliberate thought, which means your conscious attention is available to feel the groove, to hear the di-chord quality, to notice the melody's gesture. This is the logic behind Plogger's whole approach: fluency in one layer makes space for the next layer.

It is also the reason Sprint 1 does not attempt to combine complex rhythmic patterns with unfamiliar chord shapes. The rhythm work in Sprint 1 is the grid — the conceptual framework — not complex patterns. You are building the cognitive architecture. The filling-in happens as each layer becomes fluent.

## Part 3: Harmony — Introduction to Harmonic Function

### What Harmonic Function Means

There is a crucial difference between knowing what a chord contains and understanding what a chord does. A C major chord contains the notes C, E, and G. That is its content. But in a piece of music in C major, a C major chord does the job of sounding like home — like the place the music has arrived at or is trying to return to. In a piece in F major, a C major chord does a different job: it sounds like the reach away from home, the tension that will want to resolve back to F. Same chord. Same notes. Completely different function.

Harmonic function is the study of what chords do in keys — not what they are made of, but what role they play in the gravitational system of a key. Traditional theory names three primary functions, and these have been named consistently across centuries of Western music:

**Tonic** is home. A tonic chord is the center of gravity, the place of maximum stability, the chord that sounds like the question has been answered. In a major key, this is the I chord. In a minor key, it is the i chord.

**Subdominant** is departure. The subdominant chord creates a sense of movement away from home, of expansion or opening outward. It does not create urgent tension — it creates a kind of spacious distance from the tonic. In most keys, this is the IV chord (or in minor contexts, the iv or bVII, depending on the harmonic context).

**Dominant** is tension requiring return. The dominant chord creates the specific tension that the ear resolves by returning to tonic. It is not merely unstable — it is directional. A dominant chord creates a forward pull toward tonic that subdominant does not. In major keys, this is the V chord. The reason V creates such strong directional tension is largely a matter of the tritone — the [6] di-chord — buried inside a dominant seventh chord (V7), but that is Sprint 8 territory.

Understanding harmonic function transforms how you hear chord progressions. Instead of hearing "Am, then G, then Em" as a sequence of three named objects, you start hearing "home, then a step outward, then a minor version of the dominant neighbor." The names matter less than the functions. A song built on i-bVII-i in A minor is doing something functionally: establishing home, stepping out to a major chord that creates neither full departure nor dominant tension (bVII is a borrowed subdominant-area chord), and returning home. That arc has an emotional logic that the chord names alone do not convey.

The AMF is emphatic on one point about harmonic function: the organizing principle of the system is not ii-V-I. It is not even I-IV-V-I. The organizing principle is a vocabulary of fourteen di-chord root movements — the atomic intervals by which chord roots move from one to the next. I-IV-V-I is not taught as a unit to be memorized. It is arrived at naturally once you own the specific root movements that compose it. This matters because learning I-IV-V-I as a unit means you have learned one thing. Learning the [5] root movement (the interval between the root of I and the root of IV, and between the root of V and the root of I) means you have learned the atomic component from which dozens of progressions are assembled. Sprint 2 begins that work. Sprint 1 lays the conceptual groundwork.

### Minor Keys and Ain't No Sunshine

"Ain't No Sunshine" is in A minor. Understanding what this means at the functional level — rather than just as a key signature fact — is the harmonic work of Sprint 1.

A minor as tonic means that Am is home. The note A is the center of gravitational pull in this song. The chord Am — A-C-E — is the place of maximum stability. When the song returns to Am, it has arrived. Not resolved in the sense of "finished" — the song continues — but harmonically grounded, back at the center.

The minor quality of this tonic matters perceptually. The root of Am is A. The third — which determines whether the chord is major or minor — is C, which sits a [3] above A. That [3] is what you are training to hear in Sprint 1. When you hear Am and it sounds minor — dark, inward, emotionally weighted — what you are hearing is that [3] quality sitting inside the chord at the melodic level. The non-harmonic, fundamental-referring, dark character of [3] is what makes a minor chord sound minor.

The song's three chords and their functions:

**Am (i)** — Tonic minor. Home. The "I know, I know, I know" sections are built entirely on Am (or a single Am pedal). The repetition of the tonic in that passage is doing something emotionally specific: the tonic here does not feel like resolution so much as resignation. Minor tonic has that quality — stability that is also heaviness. Being home in A minor is not the same as being home in A major.

**G (bVII)** — This chord is a major chord built on the seventh scale degree of A natural minor. It is what music theory calls the subtonic or bVII chord. Its function in this key is loosely subdominant-adjacent — it moves away from the tonic toward a more open, major-quality sound. The G chord in "Ain't No Sunshine" creates the sense of the music stepping slightly outward, brightening slightly, before returning to Am. There is no urgent dominant tension in G. It is more of a brief major-quality expansion.

**Em (v) or Dm (iv)** — Depending on the arrangement you use, the third chord of the song is either Em (the minor dominant) or Dm (the minor subdominant). Em as a minor dominant creates a shadow-dominant effect — it has the root-position relationship of dominant to tonic (E is the fifth degree of A), but because it is minor (E-G-B, containing a [3] rather than a [4]), it lacks the full directional pull of a major dominant. It creates a gentler kind of tension. Dm as the minor subdominant is even quieter in tension — it simply deepens the minor quality of the harmonic world. Both are valid choices for Sprint 1. What matters is that you can hear the function: a chord that creates some motion away from the tonic Am, and leads back to it.

The emotional character of minor tonic — and this is worth sitting with, not just reading — is the reason this song works. Bill Withers is not in major. He is not sad in the way that a dominant chord wants to resolve. He is home in a minor world, and minor tonic home has a quality of settled melancholy, of weight without urgency. "Ain't No Sunshine" does not want to go anywhere. It lives in that minor-quality tonic with a kind of deliberate stillness. Understanding that the harmonic structure is enabling that emotional quality — that the [3]-based tonic is doing real emotional work, not just providing a chord to strum — is a piece of musical literacy that repays the attention.

### Root Movement as Di-Chord — The First Connection

Here is the core architectural insight of the AMF, stated directly: the movement between chord roots is a di-chord. The same perceptual vocabulary you are building in the Plogger Method — the same [3], [4], [5], [7] system — directly describes how harmony moves.

When Am resolves to G in "Ain't No Sunshine," the root moves from A down to G. A to G is one whole step down — a [2] in the di-chord system, specifically the major second. When G moves back to Am, the root moves from G up to A — again a [2], but ascending. When Am moves to Em, the root moves from A down to E — a perfect fourth down, which is a [5] di-chord (by the inversion law: a P4 down is the same interval class as a P5 up, both [5]).

You will not be asked to own these root movements in Sprint 1. [2] is Sprint 4's gate. [5] is Sprint 2's gate. But you can hear them now, and noticing them in the song you are already learning is exactly the right preparation. When you hear Am move to G in this song, your ear is encountering a [2] root movement — a whole step, the dissonant di-chord class, fast and urgent-feeling at 8Hz. That minor, restless quality of the whole step is part of why the Am-G movement in this song feels the way it does.

This connection — between the Plogger ear training and the Harmony Chamber — is not incidental. It is the architectural spine of the entire system. The Musical OS and the Harmony Chamber are not separate subjects. They are the same ear trained at different structural levels: di-chords in melody, di-chords in root movements. One system, two applications. Every di-chord you internalize as a melodic sound is also preparation for hearing harmonic motion. Every harmonic root movement you study is also a di-chord recognition exercise.

Recognizing this connection intellectually in Sprint 1 means that when you begin the real harmonic movement work in Sprint 2, it does not feel like starting over. It feels like applying something you already own to a new level of the music.

### The 14-Movement Vocabulary — A Preview

The AMF's harmonic spine is built from fourteen di-chord root movements, not from chord progressions as memorized units. This is the most important structural difference between the AMF and conventional harmony study.

The sequence of movements, organized by perceptual accessibility, begins with [5] (perfect fourth — Sprint 2), then [7] (perfect fifth — Sprint 3), [2] (major second — Sprint 4), [3] (minor third — Sprint 5), [9] (major sixth — Sprint 6), [4] (major third — Sprint 7), and [6] (tritone — Sprint 8). Sprints 9-12 extend into chromatic territory. Each sprint owns one movement deeply before adding another.

What this means for your long-term understanding: when you reach Sprint 8 and you have genuinely owned seven di-chord root movements, you can hear any diatonic progression as a combination of movements you already know. I-IV-V-I becomes [5] outward, [2] upward, [5] home — three movements, each with its own perceptual character, assembling into a familiar progression. I-V-vi-IV — the most common chord progression in contemporary music — becomes [7] (I to V), [3] (V down to vi), [9] (vi up to IV), three movements, each with its own sound.

Progressions are not memorized as patterns. They are heard as combinations of owned sounds. This is the difference between learning a sequence and understanding a language. In Sprint 1 you are not yet speaking the language — you are learning your first words. But understanding that the language has this architecture, that it is built from atomic movements rather than preset patterns, changes how you approach everything that follows.

## Part 4: Voicings — Both Instruments, First Shapes

### Why Two Instruments From Day One

The AMF studies guitar and piano simultaneously from the first session of Sprint 1. This is not a logistical convenience — it is a pedagogical argument. Each instrument illuminates the other in ways that neither can achieve alone.

The piano makes music theory physically visible. On a piano keyboard, the interval structure of a chord is a visible shape — you can see the distance between notes as a physical span across the keys. A major third ([4]) looks different from a minor third ([3]) because it is a wider span of keys. An Am chord on the piano is visibly A-C-E: the A-C span is [3], narrow; the C-E span is [4], slightly wider; the A-E span is [7], the largest. The harmonic content of the chord is laid out in space. When you understand why a minor chord sounds the way it does — because the quality-determining third is a [3] — you can see that fact on the keys. Theory becomes spatial.

The guitar makes groove physical. When you strum an Am chord on guitar, you feel the chord in your body — the weight of the pick (or thumb) moving through six strings, the vibration in the wood, the resonance of the acoustic body. The rhythmic feel of a chord is embedded in the physical act of strumming or fingerpicking. Guitar is where the groove lives in the hands. Piano is where the theory lives in the eyes and the ear. Together, they build the complete musician's apparatus: physical feel and visual-theoretical understanding reinforcing each other.

There is a third benefit to parallel study that is specific to the adult returning musician: it prevents over-reliance on habit. You have muscle memory from your previous guitar experience that is partly right and partly wrong. When you encounter the same chord on piano — where none of those habits apply — you see it fresh. You have to think about the notes, the spacing, the di-chord content. Then you return to guitar with new understanding. The two instruments keep each other honest.

### Guitar: Am, G, Em, Dm in Open Position

Open position chords are the first chord shapes on guitar because they ring fully, require relatively simple fingering, and give you direct access to the di-chord relationships between strings. Here is each chord in detail.

**Am — A minor**

The Am chord in open position is fretted with three fingers on the second fret of the second, third, and fourth strings. Specifically: the middle finger on the second fret of the fourth string (D string, producing A), the ring finger on the second fret of the third string (G string, producing A), and the index finger on the first fret of the second string (B string, producing C). The open fifth string (A) and open first string (E) are both played open.

The notes in this chord from low to high, across strings 5 through 1: A (open fifth string), A (second fret fourth string), E (open fourth string) — wait, let us be precise. Standard tuning is E-A-D-G-B-E. In the Am shape: string 5 (A) open = A, string 4 (D) at second fret = E, string 3 (G) at second fret = A, string 2 (B) at first fret = C, string 1 (E) open = E. You do not play the sixth string.

Root, third, fifth located: the root A appears on strings 5 and 3. The fifth E appears on strings 4 and 1. The third C — the note that makes this chord minor — appears on string 2. The most common voicing error with Am is pressing the second-fret notes (strings 3 and 4) with insufficient finger pressure, causing the strings to buzz or mute instead of ring. The cure is ensuring each finger falls close behind the fret wire rather than in the middle of the fret space.

The di-chord relationship between adjacent strings: string 2 to string 1 (C to E) is a [4] — major third. String 3 to string 2 (A to C) is a [3] — minor third. These are [3] and [4] sitting side by side in the chord voicing. Playing Am gives you immediate, physical access to both di-chords of Sprint 1.

**G — G major**

There are multiple valid fingerings for the open G chord. The most useful for this sprint is the "three-finger G": ring finger on the third fret of the first string (high E, producing G), middle finger on the third fret of the sixth string (low E, producing G), and index finger on the second fret of the fifth string (A, producing B). The remaining strings — D (open), G (open), and B (open) — are played open.

Notes from strings 6 to 1: G (third fret sixth string), B (second fret fifth string), D (open fourth string), G (open third string), B (open second string), G (third fret first string). Root G appears on strings 6, 3, and 1. Third B appears on strings 5 and 2. Fifth D appears on string 4.

The di-chord content: string 5 to string 4 (B to D) is a [3] — minor third. String 6 to string 5 (G to B) is a [4] — major third. There they are again: [3] and [4], this time with different gravitational roles. The [4] from G to B is the quality-determining major third (this is what makes the G chord major). The [3] from B to D is the fifth interval measured as a minor third up from the third.

The most common problem with open G is the ring and middle fingers not pressing firmly enough on the third-fret notes. If the chord sounds muted on strings 1 or 6, add pressure and ensure the fingertip is contacting the string rather than the adjacent string muting it.

**Em — E minor**

Em is the simplest open chord on guitar. Two fingers on the second fret: middle finger on the second fret of the fifth string (A string, producing B), and ring finger on the second fret of the fourth string (D string, producing E). All remaining strings are played open.

Notes from strings 6 to 1: E (open sixth string), B (second fret fifth string), E (open fourth string), G (open third string), B (open second string), E (open first string). Root E appears on strings 6, 4, and 1. Third G appears on string 3. Fifth B appears on strings 5 and 2.

The third of Em is G, a minor third above E — which is [3]. Em is minor because of the [3] between E and G. The relationship to Am: when you move from Am to Em, the root moves from A down to E — a [5] (perfect fourth down, which by the inversion law is the same as a perfect fifth up). You are hearing [5] root movement every time the song shifts between these chords.

**Dm — D minor**

Dm requires a shape change from the previous three chords. The fingering: index finger on the first fret of the first string (high E, producing F), middle finger on the second fret of the third string (G string, producing A), ring finger on the third fret of the second string (B string, producing D). The fourth string (D) is played open. Strings 5 and 6 are not played.

Notes from strings 4 to 1: D (open fourth string), A (second fret third string), D (third fret second string), F (first fret first string). Root D appears on strings 4 and 2. Third F appears on string 1. Fifth A appears on string 3.

The third of Dm is F, a minor third above D — [3] again. Every minor chord contains a [3] between its root and third. This is what your ear is learning to recognize as the minor quality sound.

The most common voicing error on Dm is accidentally catching the open third string when strumming, which produces a G (the fifth of Dm) but also blurs the distinction between the chord tones you want the ear to register. Careful strumming from the fourth string is the discipline.

**Di-chord content within chord voicings — a summary:**

Every minor chord you play contains a [3] (minor third) between its root and third. Every major chord you play contains a [4] (major third) between its root and third. This means that strumming any chord is also a [3] or [4] hearing exercise. Sprint 1's core ear training and Sprint 1's chord voicing work are not separate activities — they are the same activity at different levels of attention.

### Piano: Five-Finger Position

The five-finger position on piano — sometimes called the conjunct heptachord position — is the foundational hand position for the first several sprints. It is not just an exercise position. It is a way of mapping the scale and chord structure onto the hand, with each finger assigned to a specific scale degree, so that the hand becomes a physical representation of musical space.

In the conjunct position, both hands meet at the tonic note, each index finger on the same pitch: the tonic. From there, the right hand extends upward (toward higher pitches, away from the body) and the left hand extends downward (toward lower pitches, toward the body). The index fingers share the tonic. The middle fingers play the second degree (right) and seventh degree (left). The ring fingers play the third degree (right) and sixth degree (left). The pinkies play the fourth degree (right) and fifth degree (left).

The finger-to-degree mapping stays constant regardless of mode or key:

| Fingers | Right Hand | Left Hand |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Index | Tonic (1) | Tonic (1) |
| Middle | 2nd degree | 7th degree |
| Ring | 3rd degree | 6th degree |
| Pinky | 4th degree | 5th degree |

This assignment has a consequence you will feel immediately: the ring finger always plays the third degree of the scale. The ring finger of the right hand — your weakest non-pinky finger — is the finger that determines major or minor quality. In C major, the right ring finger rests on E (a [4] from C). In A minor, the right ring finger rests on C (a [3] from A). The physical sensation under your ring finger encodes the quality of the mode.

For Sprint 1, establish two positions: C major (tonic on middle C for the right index, C below middle C for the left index) and A minor (tonic on A above middle C for the right index, A below middle C for the left index). In A minor, the di-chord content of the hand position is visible and physical: the span from A to C under the right index and ring fingers is three semitones — [3], the minor third, the quality di-chord you are training to hear.

The Zone 1 notes — root, third, fifth — are directly playable in this position without shifting. For Am, the root A is under the index finger, the third C is under the ring finger, and the fifth E requires the right hand middle finger to stretch — but the fifth E is within the position span. For C major, the root C is under the index, the third E is under the ring, and the fifth G is within reach of the pinky.

What makes the piano position valuable for di-chord training is that you can play any two notes in the position and observe the visual span between them on the keys. A minor third ([3]) between A and C spans two white keys with one black key between them. A major third ([4]) between C and E spans two white keys with two black keys between them. The visual difference is there. The physical distance is there. Playing and seeing the di-chord simultaneously accelerates recognition.

### Parallel Practice

Playing the same chord on both instruments in the same practice session creates a specific kind of learning that neither instrument alone produces. The chord's di-chord content is the same on both instruments — Am is A-C-E regardless of whether you are playing a guitar or a keyboard. But the physical experience of the chord is completely different.

On guitar, Am is a grip: three fingers pressing strings, a particular resistance, vibration moving through wood and air. The sound blooms and decays. On piano, Am is three keys pressed: a cleaner attack, a more uniform sustain, the chord laid out visually as a span across the keyboard. The guitar Am is felt in the body. The piano Am is seen in space.

When you move between the two instruments and find the same [3] quality — the same minor sound — in both, you are reinforcing the di-chord as a perceptual object independent of any physical medium. The [3] between A and C is not "the guitar sound of minor" or "the piano sound of minor." It is a di-chord that exists in both. Your ear begins to hear it as the thing itself, rather than as an instrument-specific sensation. This is the beginning of genuine musical abstraction — hearing in di-chords rather than in instrument gestures.

## Part 5: Rhythm — The Binary Grid and Longy

### How the Rhythm Code Grid Works

The Rhythm Code system begins with a foundational claim that departs from conventional rhythm teaching: rhythm is not about duration values. It is about starting points.

When you read a quarter note in traditional notation, you are reading a duration: a note that lasts for one beat. The Rhythm Code does not think about rhythm this way. Instead of asking "how long does this note last," it asks "which of the eight available positions in this measure does this note start on?" The duration follows from the starting position and the next event. The note lasts until something else happens.

This reframing has a practical consequence: instead of reading rhythmic values (quarters, eighths, dotted quarters, syncopations), you are reading a grid of eight positions, each of which is either active (a note starts here) or inactive (no new note starts here). The grid is binary. On or off. Eight positions per measure in 4/4 time, each position representing one eighth note.

The eight positions correspond to familiar rhythmic landmarks:

**Position 1** is the downbeat — beat one. This is the gravitational center of the measure, the heaviest and most stable position. Music that consistently starts events on position 1 sounds grounded and declarative.

**Position 2** is the "and" of beat one — the eighth note upbeat between beats one and two.

**Position 3** is beat two.

**Position 4** is the "and" of beat two — the position that sits between beats two and three.

**Position 5** is beat three. In many groove contexts, positions 1 and 5 together form the backbeat of the measure.

**Position 6** is the "and" of beat three.

**Position 7** is beat four.

**Position 8** is the "and" of beat four — the last eighth note before the measure repeats. This position is the most rhythmically charged in the grid. A note that starts on position 8 is arriving one eighth note before the next downbeat — it is anticipating the following measure. This is one of the most important groove concepts in the entire system.

**Stops** are one of the two key rhythmic devices the Rhythm Code introduces at this level. A stop is the last note before a silence — the note at which the phrase "lands." The stop determines where the rhythmic phrase ends, and where you choose to stop dramatically affects the feel of the music. Stopping on position 1 feels declarative and final. Stopping on position 3 or 7 creates a certain hanging quality. The stop concept is what makes "Ain't No Sunshine" immediately useful as a rhythm study: that song is built on stops — on abrupt silences that are as musically active as the notes themselves.

**Anticipations** are notes that start slightly before the beat they "belong to" — arriving early, creating forward momentum. The regular anticipation is an eighth note early: a note that "belongs to" beat one of the next measure starts on position 8 of the current measure. The quarter-note anticipation is available only from beat four: it can start as early as position 6.

The grid notation system writes these as an eight-position binary sequence, either with numbers for the positions that are active or with a graphical representation showing on and off positions. For Sprint 1, you do not need the full notation — you need the concept. Eight positions. Each either active or inactive. Stops and anticipations as the two primary rhythmic shapes.

One measure of a basic groove in the Rhythm Code system — say, the most elementary version of a quarter-note pattern — looks like this: positions 1, 3, 5, 7 active. Every other position inactive. That is four quarter notes in 4/4. The simplest possible groove. From here, you add anticipations (move some events to the preceding eighth note), define stops (let some events be the last note before silence), and begin to shape the groove toward something that feels alive rather than metronomic.

### Longy: ta and ta-te

The Longy system — named for Emile-Joseph Longy, a French clarinetist who codified it in the nineteenth century — is a syllabic encoding of rhythm. It is not the same as the familiar "one-and-two-and" counting system, and the difference matters.

Counting numbers and subdivisions tracks position. "One-and-two-and" tells you where you are in the measure. The Longy syllables encode duration. **ta** is a single undivided beat — a quarter note in simple time. **ta-te** is a beat divided into two equal parts — two eighth notes. The syllable "ta" cannot be rushed because saying "ta" takes a certain amount of time. The syllable pair "ta-te" contains an "and" built into it, tying the second syllable to the first and encoding the equal division of the beat.

This is the core insight of the Longy system: the syllable carries the duration. You cannot say "ta" too quickly and still produce the correct rhythm — the physical act of pronouncing the syllable occupies time. When you can speak a rhythmic pattern fluently using Longy syllables, you can feel whether you are rushing or dragging in a way that abstract counting does not provide, because counting is positional (where are you) while Longy is temporal (how long does this last).

In Sprint 1 you are working with exactly two Longy patterns: **ta** and **ta-te**. Everything else in the Longy system is built from these two foundations. Ta is the quarter note: one beat, undivided. Ta-te is the pair of eighth notes: one beat divided into two equal halves. Four ta's in a row is four quarter notes — a full measure of the simplest pulse. Two ta-te patterns is a measure of all eighth notes. And the combinations — ta ta-te ta, ta-te ta ta, and so on — begin to produce the rhythmic variety that groove is made of.

The Longy system and the Rhythm Code grid operate at different levels and both are necessary. Longy answers: what is this rhythm, and how is it notated? The Rhythm Code answers: does this rhythm feel good, and where does it sit in the groove? You need the notation literacy that Longy provides to communicate about rhythm in any formal context. You need the groove awareness that the Rhythm Code provides to know whether the rhythm is actually working in a musical context. Sprint 1 introduces both so that they can develop in parallel from the beginning.

### The Rhythm of Ain't No Sunshine

"Ain't No Sunshine" has one of the most instructive rhythmic structures in the popular song repertoire for a beginning groove study, specifically because of its use of silence.

The famous "I know" section — approximately twenty-six repetitions of the phrase "I know" — is rhythmically remarkable. It is not a section where Bill Withers is playing through changes. It is a section built on silence and the beat. Between each "I know," there is a rhythmic gap — a stop. The phrase starts on a downbeat-adjacent position and releases before the next beat lands fully. The stops are not empty. They are rhythmically active silences. The rhythm of the "I know" section lives as much in what is not played as in what is.

This is the most direct possible introduction to the concept of the stop. In the Rhythm Code language: each "I know" starts on a specific grid position and stops before the next strong position. The silence is a rhythmic event. The listener's ear fills in the beat, continues the pulse internally, and experiences the next "I know" as arriving exactly where it should — the rhythmic expectation set up by the stops is the groove.

The song's overall feel is moderately slow, somewhere around 80-90 BPM, in 4/4 time. The groove is in the bass line and rhythm guitar, not in a strummed pattern. The basic strumming pattern that works for the chord changes section is built around positions 1 and 5 — downbeats of beats one and three — with anticipations moving chord changes slightly early. The stop-time feel of the "I know" section uses the grid's position 1 as the landing point for each phrase, with positions 2-8 silent until the next "I know" arrives.

On the 8-position grid, a single measure of the "I know" section might look like: position 1 active (the "I"), positions 2-8 inactive (the silence before the next "know"), position 1 of the next measure active again. The simplicity of this pattern is its power. Understanding why it grooves — why those silences feel heavy and purposeful rather than merely empty — is a question the Rhythm Code is designed to answer. The stops are the answer. You are not just waiting for the next note. You are in the middle of a rhythmic phrase that the silence is completing.

## Part 6: The Anchor Song — Ain't No Sunshine

"Ain't No Sunshine" was written and recorded by Bill Withers in 1971, appearing on his debut album Just As I Am. It runs slightly over two minutes. It received very little initial promotion and became a hit almost entirely through radio airplay. For this curriculum, it was chosen as the Sprint 1 anchor song for reasons that are worth stating precisely, because understanding why a song was chosen illuminates how to use it.

**Di-chord content in the melody and bass:**

The melody sits almost entirely in Zone 1 — chord tones of whatever chord is currently sounding. This is not accidental. Bill Withers was not a trained musician in the academic sense; he was a self-taught writer who arrived at his melodic choices through instinct and repetition, and those instincts consistently landed him on the most stable, emotionally resonant notes. Zone 1 notes in a minor tonic sound emotionally weighted precisely because they are acoustically resolved while being harmonically minor.

The primary di-chord movement in the bass is the [2] — the whole-step relationship between Am and G (root A down to root G, or root G up to root A). This is a dissonant di-chord (8Hz, jagged wave), which means there is a slightly urgent quality to the Am-G oscillation even though neither chord is what traditional theory would call "dissonant." The urgency of [2] is why the Am-G movement in this song has a restless, aching quality despite the slow tempo. The bass interval itself is doing emotional work.

The Am-Em movement (or Am-Dm, depending on arrangement) covers a [5] root movement — a perfect fourth downward (A to E) or a perfect fifth downward (A to D). The [5] di-chord has a perfect pulsation (2Hz, straight wave), which means the movement is acoustically stable — not urgent. This gives the Am-to-minor-third-chord movement a sense of settling deeper into the minor world, not straining against it.

**Harmonic function analysis:**

The song operates in A natural minor / Aeolian mode. The chord that receives the most time is Am (i) — tonic minor. The G chord (bVII) functions as a subtonic, a borrowed major chord from the parallel major that creates a momentary brightening before the return to minor. The Em or Dm functions as a minor dominant or minor subdominant.

What is notable about the harmonic language is its absence of dominant-function tension. There is no E major chord (the actual V chord of A minor, which would contain G-sharp and create strong leading-tone tension). The song never pushes hard toward resolution — it pools in tonic minor, moves briefly outward, and returns. This harmonic restraint is part of what gives the song its emotional texture: resignation without release. The harmonic structure is saying "this is where we live" rather than "we need to get somewhere."

**Zone 1 melody in the song:**

The verse melody of "Ain't No Sunshine" centers on A and C — the root and minor third of Am. The phrase "Ain't no sunshine when she's gone" moves primarily between A and C with E appearing as a structural articulation. This is Zone 1 melody at its most direct: root, third, fifth, in a minor tonic context. Withers is not avoiding the chord tones — he is leaning into them, letting the [3] quality of the minor third carry the emotional weight.

The "I know" section abandons melodic development entirely and becomes a rhythmic incantation on a single pitch. The note he uses is A — the root. A phrase of twenty-six repetitions of "I know" on the tonic root, over the Am chord, is Zone 1 reduced to its simplest element. This is one reason the section is so powerful: it is harmonically resolved (root over tonic chord) but rhythmically and dynamically accumulating. The stops do the work.

**Rhythmic structure and grid mapping:**

The main groove of the song uses the following position pattern (approximate, as the performance has feel variation): positions 1 and 5 are the primary downbeat anchors. The Am chord change often anticipates beat one by an eighth note — position 8 of the preceding measure. This is the quarter-note anticipation from beat four, one of the most common groove moves in soul and R&B.

The "I know" section, as discussed in Part 5, is built on rhythmic stops. Each "I know" can be mapped to roughly position 1 (downbeat) with a stop before position 3 — the silence is one to two beats long before the next phrase starts. The exact placement varies across Withers's performance — and that variation, that slight flexibility in where each "I know" lands, is part of what makes it feel like a human being rather than a machine.

**Why this song for Sprint 1:**

The case for "Ain't No Sunshine" as the Sprint 1 anchor song comes down to four properties that align with what Sprint 1 is trying to establish:

First, it is unambiguously minor. There is no confusion about the tonal center or the quality. A minor is home, it sounds like home, and the [3]-based quality of the tonic chord is the most prominent di-chord in the song's harmonic landscape. You will spend your Sprint 1 practice hours living inside the sound that Sprint 1's Plogger work is training you to hear.

Second, the harmonic structure is simple enough that you can absorb the song with three chords. You are not navigating complexity. The song earns its emotional weight through rhythmic design and melodic restraint, not harmonic sophistication. This leaves your attention free for the things Sprint 1 asks you to attend to: di-chord quality, Zone 1 melody, rhythm grid.

Third, it grooves in a way that makes repetition pleasurable. The stop-time structure creates rhythmic interest without requiring technical complexity in the accompaniment. A student who can play three chords in open position can play "Ain't No Sunshine" in a way that feels musical, not merely correct.

Fourth, it is worth playing. You will play this song dozens of times before Sprint 1 ends. It needs to reward that repeated engagement. "Ain't No Sunshine" does. There is real musical substance here — Withers's phrasing, the rhythmic design, the emotional arc of the song — that repays attention over time.

**What you should be able to do with this song by sprint end:**

The specific evidence that you own Sprint 1 in "Ain't No Sunshine" is this: you can sit down, without preparation, and play a simplified but musical version of the song on both guitar and piano. Not perfectly. Fluently. You can hear when you arrive at Am that you are home in a minor world. You can hear the [3] quality of the minor tonic chord and know what you are hearing. You can feel the stops in the "I know" section as rhythmic events, not as silences waiting to be filled. And you can locate Zone 1 — the A, C, and E of Am — under your fingers on both instruments without hunting.

## Closing: What Owning Sprint 1 Feels Like

Stage 3 on the Sprint 1 material is not a checklist state. It is a qualitative shift in how you move through musical experience. Here is what it actually feels like.

When you hear a minor chord — in a song, in a film, in a passing radio advertisement — you will notice a quality. Not think about it. Notice it. The [3] character of the minor third will register the way a color registers visually — immediately, without deliberate analysis. The closed, dark, non-harmonic quality of [3] will be legible to you as minor quality, and you will not need to work to read it. This is different from knowing intellectually that minor chords contain minor thirds. Stage 3 means the perception arrives before the thought.

When you play Am on guitar or piano, you will feel the chord as a sound-object with specific properties, not just as a shape to reproduce. The voicings are not just fingerings — they are clusters of di-chord relationships that you can hear as you play them. The [3] between the root and third, the [7] between the root and fifth, the [4] between the third and fifth. These are not analytical observations you are making during practice. They are acoustic facts that are present in the sound, and your ear has begun to register them.

The 8-position grid has become the way you hear rhythm, not a concept you apply to rhythm. When a song plays, you can feel where events are landing on the grid. Stops register as stops — you hear the silence as a rhythmic choice rather than a gap. Anticipations feel like anticipations — a note arriving slightly early, creating forward pull.

"Ain't No Sunshine" can be played without stopping. Not perfectly — mistakes are fine at Stage 3 on a sprint. But without the hesitations and resets that characterize Stage 2. You can play it from start to finish, and somewhere in the playing there will be a moment — probably in the "I know" section, probably in the stops — where you stop thinking about the song and start hearing it. That moment, when the framework temporarily disappears and you are just playing music, is the north star of the entire AMF. Sprint 1 is not the destination. But it is the first time you will feel the direction.

When you move to Sprint 2, the [5] root movement will not feel like starting over. It will feel like adding one more di-chord to a vocabulary you are already building. The gate system will feel less like an evaluation and more like a honest reckoning with what you actually own versus what you have merely encountered. And the fact that you cannot rush the gate — that Stage 2 ("I can do it slowly") is explicitly not the standard — will make sense in a way it may not have when you first read it. Stage 3 is not more practice. It is a different kind of presence. Sprint 1 is your first taste of what it means to work toward it.
