The Functional Inversions — Real-World Applications
C/E
I/3rd — Tonic Softener
Bass: C → E → F
Softens I chord when scale degree 3 is in the melody. Creates a descending or stepwise bass line. Gentle, flowing.
G/B
V/7th — Leading Tone Bass
"Man in the Mirror" (MJ)
Bass: G/B → C
B rises stepwise to C — most elegant possible bass resolution. Dominant function preserved but smoother approach.
Em/B
iii/5th — Melancholic 5th
"Shape of My Heart" (Sting)
Bass: G → Em/B → C
Most melancholic harmonization of scale degree 5. The B sustains, creating introspective minor flavor before release.
Why Invert?
Smooth Bass Lines
Inversions allow the bass to move stepwise (C-E-F-G) instead of jumping between roots. This creates more elegant, singable bass motion.
Voice Leading Efficiency
When one voice needs to hold a note or move by a small interval, inverting a chord keeps all voices moving minimally — the "law of least motion."
Harmonic Variety
The same chord in root, 1st, and 2nd inversion feel different. Inversions add harmonic variety without changing the overall harmonic function or chord quality.
