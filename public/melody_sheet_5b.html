Chord Change Pillar Notes

How to choose the melody note that anchors each chord section — and what effect each choice creates
A pillar note is the melody note you deliberately place at a chord change — the note that anchors the new chord section. Every chord change in a song is an opportunity to choose: do you resolve, create forward motion, or escalate tension?
How to Choose Your Pillar Notes — 3 Rules
1
Smooth Handoff
Choose a note that IS in the incoming chord for a smooth, supported landing at the change. The listener feels the chord change happen without friction.
2
Forward Motion
Choose an unstable chord tone (the 7th of a dominant chord, the 3rd of a tense chord) for grounded tension with forward pull. The chord catches the note but it still wants to move.
3
Maximum Clarity
Choose the root of the new chord for maximum clarity and a decisive arrival. Strongest resolution — makes the chord change unmistakable and confident.
Pillar Note Table — Common Chords in C Major

C DO (root) Grounded anchor — "we've arrived" TI Restless even at rest — pulls away from the chord
G7 SO or TI Irresistible pull toward home — dominant tension FA Extreme tension — FA over G7 is the loudest alarm bell in the key
Am LA (root) Dark, bittersweet arrival — minor tonic feeling DO Floats above the minor — unstable, searching quality
F FA (root) or DO Warm, spacious arrival — F is the "breath" chord TI Bittersweet friction — TI clashes with the subdominant
Voice Leading Tip — Stepwise Pillar Notes
DO → TI → LA → SO
Pillar notes that move by step (or half-step) between chord changes create smooth, singable lines that feel inevitable. This descending stepwise motion (DO→TI→LA→SO) is one of the most common patterns in pop and soul — it gives the melody a natural gravitational flow even as the chords shift underneath.
