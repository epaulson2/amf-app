7th & 6th Chords

Adding a 7th to any triad creates a richer 4-note sound. The type of 7th determines everything about function and emotional color.
The 6 Seventh Chord Types (all from C)
Major 7th
Maj7
C–E–G–B
CΔ7 or Cmaj7
LushDreamyStable
Major triad + major 7th. The 7th is only a half step below the root — creates beautiful tension that still feels resolved. Neo-soul, jazz, R&B staple.
Dominant 7th
Dom7
C–E–G–B♭
C7
TenseDrivingTritone
Major triad + minor 7th. The E and B♭ form a tritone (THE engine of dominant function). The most harmonically active chord — must resolve. All V chords are dom7.
Minor 7th
Min7
C–E♭–G–B♭
Cm7
MellowJazzyIntrospective
Minor triad + minor 7th. Soft and introspective — the 7th smooths the minor sound. Used for ii chords in the ii-V-I. Standard jazz and neo-soul texture chord.
Half-Diminished
Half-Dim
C–E♭–G♭–B♭
Cø7 or Cm7♭5
DarkYearningMinor ii
Diminished triad + minor 7th. The ii chord in minor key ii-V-i. Darker than minor 7th but not fully tense like full-dim. Used extensively in jazz standards.
Full Diminished
Dim7
C–E♭–G♭–A
C°7
TenseSpookySymmetrical
Diminished triad + diminished 7th (doubly diminished). Three stacked m3s — perfectly symmetrical. Only 3 unique sounds. Functions as rootless dominant 7♭9.
Minor Major 7th
m(Δ7)
C–E♭–G–B
Cm(Δ7) or CmM7
Dark + BrightSpy MovieComplex
Minor triad + major 7th. The half-step between the maj7 (B) and the root (C) creates acute tension inside a dark minor frame. Dramatic, complex, cinematic. "Spy movie chord."

6th Chords — The Hidden Enharmonic Identity
Major 6 (C6)
C–E–G–A
"Stable, vintage, complete"
C6 = Am7 (same notes, different root)
Minor 6 (Cm6)
C–E♭–G–A
"Dark, sophisticated, jazz"
Cm6 = Bø7 in 1st inversion (B–E♭–G–C)
