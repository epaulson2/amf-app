Clave Variations

Son Clave

M1: 1 0 0 1 0 1 0 0

Most common pattern. Foundation of salsa, pop, funk. Five notes maximally distributed across two measures.
Rumba Clave

M1: 1 0 0 1 0 0 1 0

Third note shifted one position later. Used in Michael Jackson's "Thriller." Slightly heavier, more grounded feel.
Bossa Nova Clave

Brazilian variant, 5 notes over 2 bars

Found in "Girl from Ipanema" (João Gilberto) and "Maple Leaf Rag" (Scott Joplin). Light, flowing feel.
History: The Rhythm That Conquered the World
First written record: Baghdad, 1258 AD — Safi al-Din's "Kitāb al-Adwār." The pattern was already established music theory in the Islamic world over 750 years ago.

African roots: The same pattern appears in Ghana's kpanlogo music and multiple West African percussion traditions — predating its written documentation by centuries.

Today: Found in ALL genres — pop, hip-hop, rock, country, EDM, jazz, classical, metal. It is mathematically optimal: the most even distribution of 5 notes across 16 positions. The listener's brain finds it naturally satisfying.
