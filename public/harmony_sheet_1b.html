Interval Quality System

How intervals are classified by quality, how quality changes, and the symmetry of interval inversions.
Quality Progressions
Major / Minor Intervals (2nds, 3rds, 6ths, 7ths)
dimDiminished
minMinor
MAJMajor
augAugmented
lower 1 semitone lower 1 semitone raise 1 semitone raise 1 semitone
Major is the baseline. Lower by one half-step → Minor. Lower again → Diminished. Raise from Major by one half-step → Augmented.
Perfect Intervals (4ths, 5ths, Unison, Octave)
dimDiminished
PERFPerfect
augAugmented
Perfect intervals skip the Major/Minor distinction entirely. Perfect is the baseline. No "major" or "minor" — only Perfect, Diminished, or Augmented.
P4 raised = Aug4 = Tritone. This is the dividing line between the two systems.
The Inversion Rule
Original + Inversion = 9
Flip any interval upside down — the two numbers always add up to 9. The quality flips too (except Perfect).

Why "Perfect"? — The Overtone Series
Perfect intervals (P4, P5, P8) appear very early in the natural overtone series — the physical overtones that ring when any note is played. They sound maximally open and stable because they ARE the building blocks of sound itself. Power chords in rock use only P5 + P8 because there's no 3rd to define major or minor — just pure, culturally universal power. The "Perfect" name reflects their privileged acoustic status.
