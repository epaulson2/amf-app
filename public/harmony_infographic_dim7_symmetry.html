The Dim7 Symmetry

There are only 3 unique diminished 7th sounds in all of Western music. The other 9 dim7 chord names are inversions of these same 3 sets of notes.

What This Means in Practice
You're memorizing 3, not 12
If you know C°7, E♭°7, G♭°7, and A°7 are all the same sound — you only need to learn the fingering once and rotate it. 12 dim7 chord names → 3 unique sounds.
Rootless dominant 7♭9
Every dim7 chord implies a dominant 7♭9. G♯°7 = E7♭9 without the E. This is why dim7 chords resolve like dominants — they ARE dominants, just without the root stated.
4 chord tones = 4 possible dominants
C°7 can function as C7♭9, E♭7♭9, G♭7♭9, or A7♭9 depending on which note you treat as the leading tone. One voicing, four contextual uses.
Inversions sound identical
Because the intervals are perfectly equal, every inversion of a dim7 chord sounds like a root position chord in a different key. No inversion sounds "different" — they're all equally valid root positions.
