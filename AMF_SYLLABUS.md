## 1. What This Course Is

The Adaptable Musician's Framework (AMF) is a structured curriculum for adult musicians who want to reach genuine musical fluency — not the ability to perform one set of pieces, but the ability to play what you hear in your head in real time.

It is built on a single organizing principle: **perceptual mastery before technique.** You train your ears first. Your hands follow what your ears already know. By the time a chord shape or rhythmic pattern reaches your fingers, your brain already owns it as a sound.

The AMF runs **guitar and piano simultaneously** from day one. Not "learn piano first, then guitar" — both instruments, every sprint, every session. Each instrument illuminates the other. Piano makes the theory visible. Guitar makes the groove physical.

**What this course produces after Year 1:**
- A musician who can hear a chord movement in a song and identify it without thinking
- A musician who can play 5-8 songs at performance level (from memory, at tempo, in front of someone, without stopping)
- A musician who understands why progressions feel the way they feel — not just what they are called
- A musician who has internalized a practice system they can apply to any new material independently

## 2. Who It's For

Adult returning musicians: people who played an instrument (or sang) at some point in their life and want to return — now with the time, resources, and intentionality to do it properly. Not beginners. Not working professionals. The adult who left music behind and is coming back.

**You are the right fit if:**
- You can already read music at a basic level, or you once could and it will come back fast
- You are prepared to practice daily (45-60 min/day minimum) rather than casually weekly
- You own or have access to both a guitar and a piano/keyboard
- You are willing to go slow enough to actually own each skill before moving on

**This course is not:**
- A "learn guitar in 30 days" shortcut
- Designed for complete beginners with no prior music exposure
- A performance preparation course for a specific recital
- A course that teaches theory as an end in itself — theory here is always in service of hearing and playing

## 3. The Founding Insight: Harmonic Atoms

Most harmony courses teach progressions as units: "learn the I-IV-V-I." The AMF takes a different approach.

**Every chord progression is built from harmonic movements — the bass moving from one root to another.** That movement between roots is an interval. That interval is a di-chord. The same perceptual vocabulary you build in ear training (Section 1 of every session) maps directly onto how harmony moves.

When the bass moves from I to IV, it moves up a perfect 4th. That is a [5] di-chord.  
When the bass moves from I to V, it moves up a perfect 5th. That is a [7] di-chord.  
When V resolves back to I, the bass moves up a perfect 4th again. Still [5].

This means **the Musical OS (Plogger ear training) and the Harmony Chamber are not separate systems — they are the same ear trained at different levels.** Every harmonic movement you drill is a di-chord recognition exercise. Every di-chord you identify by ear is preparation for hearing harmonic motion in real time.

**The minimum harmonic movement principle:**  
Rather than learning a full progression as a single unit, AMF sequences learning by the smallest ownable harmonic atom — one di-chord root movement per sprint. Movements are introduced in order of perceptual simplicity: the most stable, most frequently occurring intervals first. By the time a student learns the more complex movements, they already own the components that make up every major progression in Western music.

Students do not "learn I-V-vi-IV as a progression." They arrive at it naturally — because they already own [7] (I→V), [3] (I→vi), and [9] (vi→IV) as separate sounds they can identify by ear in real time. The progression is not taught; it is assembled from owned parts.

## 4. How Progress Works: Mastery Gates, Not Calendars

The AMF is **mastery-gated**, not time-gated. A sprint is complete when you own its target skills — not when a month has passed.

Some sprints take 3 weeks. Some take 6. Both are correct outcomes.

### What "Owned" Means — The Stage 3 Standard

A skill is **not owned** until all five of these are true:

| Criterion | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **1. Correct without hesitation** | At musical tempo — not "slowly and carefully" |
| **2. Both instruments** | Guitar AND piano — not just the easier one |
| **3. At least 3 keys** | Not just the anchor song's key |
| **4. By ear in real time** | Can identify it in a song without preparation |
| **5. Used expressively** | Not just reproduced academically — it makes music |

### Stage Labels

| Stage | Name | What It Looks Like |
|-------|------|--------------------|
| **Stage 1** | Discovering | First contact. Errors expected and correct. Do not stop at errors. |
| **Stage 2** | Consolidating | Sometimes know it. Slow, deliberate repetition. Getting there. |
| **Stage 3** | Fluent | Always correct, at tempo, without conscious attention. **This is the gate.** |

"I can do it slowly" is Stage 2. Stage 2 is not the gate.

### Two Gate Types Run in Parallel

**Gate A — Harmonic Root Movement:** One di-chord root movement owned per sprint (both instruments, 3+ keys, by ear).  
**Gate B — Plogger Chapter Mastery:** One chapter or chapter group owned per sprint.

These gates can advance at different rates. If Gate A is met in week 3 but Gate B isn't, you keep working the Plogger material. The sprint does not end until both gates are cleared.

## 5. The Two-Tier Structure

### Tier 1 — Spiral Threads
Skills that start small and deepen forever. You never finish these. Every sprint adds a layer. See [Section 10](#10-spiral-threads--what-deepens-every-sprint) for the full thread map.

### Tier 2 — Mastery Gates
One discrete skill per sprint — the gate. You do not advance until this is genuinely owned on both instruments.

Every sprint produces two documents:

## 6. The Daily Practice Session

Every session follows the same five-section structure. Total: ~60 minutes.

| Section | Time | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| **1 — Plogger Foundation** | 10 min | Isolated Plogger chapter work — ear training, di-chord perception |
| **2 — Integrated Work** | 18 min | 2-3 multi-layered exercises combining all active spiral threads |
| **3 — Song Learning / Practice** | 14 min | Anchor song as recognition and performance proof |
| **4 — Jamming / Free Play** | 10 min | Structured improv using only owned materials — no new material |
| **5 — The Synthesizer** | 8 min | All four chambers simultaneously — this is the convergence zone |

**Rules for Section 2 (Integrated Work):**  
- Never more than 3 exercises per session  
- Each exercise must serve at least 3 purposes simultaneously  
- The sprint's target di-chord movement is always the center of gravity  
- Hear and sing every exercise before you play it

**Rules for Section 4 (Free Play):**  
- Only owned material — nothing from the current sprint's Stage 1/2 skills  
- No stopping at mistakes — mistakes are data, not failures  
- The anchor song is always available as a home base

**Rules for Section 5 (The Synthesizer):**  
- All four chambers operating together  
- Not a practice section — a performance section  
- If you can't synthesize it yet, you haven't owned it yet

## 7. The Di-Chord Movement Sequence

Movements are introduced in order of **perceptual simplicity** — the most stable and most common intervals first. Each sprint adds exactly one movement. By Sprint 8, a student who has genuinely mastered all 7 movements can assemble any diatonic progression by ear.

| Sprint | Di-Chord | Interval | Primary Root Movements | What You're Learning to Hear |
|--------|----------|----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| 2 | **[5]** | P4 up / P5 down | I→IV (opens outward), V→I (returns home) | The same interval in two directions — one reaches toward subdominant, one returns home. Resolution is [5]. |
| 3 | **[7]** | P5 up / P4 down | I→V (the dominant reach) | The farthest stable reach before needing to return — dominant motion |
| 4 | **[2]** | M2 up | IV→V (tension builder), V→vi (deceptive) | The smallest step up — tension that urgently wants to release |
| 5 | **[3]** | m3 down | I→vi (darkening) | The relative minor shift — same notes, different gravity center |
| 6 | **[9]** | M6 up | vi→IV (emotional lift) | The inverse of [3] — brightness opening after the minor pull |
| 7 | **[4]** | M3 up | I→iii (mediant) | The bittersweet third — neither fully major nor minor in feel |
| 8 | **[6]** | Tritone | V7→I (tritone resolution) | The most tense interval resolving — the full explanation of dominant function |

**Sprints 9-12 (to be designed):** Higher-order movements including chromatic approaches, secondary dominants, modal borrowing, and altered dominant function — all built on the owned [2]–[7] movement vocabulary.

**Important:** Sprints 2-8 do not ignore the other movements — you hear them in songs, in examples, in The Synthesizer. But the gate, the exercises, and the focused drill center on the sprint's one target movement. Everything else is "not yet" material that your ear is passively filing for later.

The 12+2 progressions are **not lesson content — they are proof exercises.** A student does not learn I-V-vi-IV as a unit. They arrive at it because they own its component movements.

## 11. The Four Chambers

All four chambers run simultaneously from Sprint 1. They are not sequential stages.

### Musical OS — The Plogger Method
The perceptual spine. Di-chords ([1]–[11]) as the 11 audible interval classes. Three sound factors for each:
- **Pulsation:** How fast the acoustic interference wave beats (8Hz dissonant → 4Hz modal → 2Hz perfect)
- **F/O Factor:** Shadow quality — how much the interval "looms" (Full: heavy presence; Open: airy; Neutral: neither)
- **Harmonicity:** Color quality — how the overtones blend (Sharp, Warm, Mellow, Bright, Hollow, etc.)

The Plogger Method teaches these by ear, not by notation. You learn to hear each di-chord as a sound-object with a specific feel before you ever see it on a staff.

### Harmony Chamber
Root movement di-chords as the organizing principle (see Sections 3 and 7). The 14-movement set is the spine. Progressions are assembled from owned movements, not taught as units.

### Voicings Chamber
How chords are physically constructed on both instruments. Progresses from open position → root position triads → dominant 7ths → shell voicings → Drop 2 → four-voice voice leading → moveable shapes.

### Rhythm Chamber
The Rhythm Code (8-position binary grid), Longy solfege, and Son Clave as three complementary systems. Groove development as a 4-step process from notated patterns to self-generated feel.

## 12. Design Non-Negotiables

These do not change regardless of how the curriculum is restructured.

| # | Principle |
|---|-----------|
| **1** | **ii-V-I is a drill vehicle, not the harmonic spine.** The 14-movement set is the spine. Never let ii-V-I dominate curriculum framing. |

| **3** | **Hear → Sing → Play** in every exercise, every section, every sprint. No exceptions. |
| **4** | **Few exercises, multi-layered** — 2-3 per section, each serving 3-4 purposes simultaneously. No single-purpose drills. |
| **5** | **The anchor song threads every section** — it is not a separate activity, it is the evidence layer. |
| **6** | **"The framework disappears"** is the north star. Every design decision must point toward eventual internalization. |
| **7** | **Mastery over pace.** The sprint calendar is a guide, not a contract. |
| **8** | **No country** in repertoire or examples. |
